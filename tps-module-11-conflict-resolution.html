The Escalation Ladder:
Read the Level Before You Choose the Response

⏱ 22 min
🔥 Friedrich Glasl · Escalation Stages · Intervention Matching · The Diagnostic Foundation

The single most common conflict resolution error in fire service supervision is not choosing the wrong response — it is choosing a response before diagnosing what level of conflict you are actually dealing with. The same conversation that resolves a Level 2 conflict can make a Level 5 conflict catastrophically worse. The formal discipline process that is exactly right for Level 6 is wildly disproportionate for Level 2. And the collaborative dialogue approach that produces beautiful results at Level 2 is dangerously naive at Level 5.

Friedrich Glasl, an Austrian organizational theorist, mapped nine stages of conflict escalation arranged in three zones. The zones determine everything: who intervenes, how they intervene, what a successful outcome looks like, and what interventions are available. Most company officers have never been taught this distinction — they have one or two conflict approaches and apply them to every situation regardless of the level. The result is that they consistently over- or under-respond, and the conflict either escalates unnecessarily or gets pushed below the surface where it compounds.

The Three Zones — The Most Important Conflict Diagnostic Available

1
Zone 1 — Win-Win Still Possible · Green Zone
Hardening of Positions
Parties begin to form fixed views and lose flexibility. Conversation becomes more rigid. Occasional tension but still fundamentally collaborative. The issue can be named and addressed directly without significant defensiveness. This is where most fire service conflict should be resolved — and where most supervisors wait too long to act.
Tool: Direct conversation · CALM · DESC if asserting a standard
2
Zone 1 — Win-Win Still Possible · Green Zone
Debates and Polemics
Arguments replace conversations. Each party is trying to win rather than solve. Misinterpretation increases. Neither party is genuinely listening to the other — each is waiting for the other to finish speaking. The relationship is still basically intact but under visible strain. Interests can still be found beneath the competing positions.
Tool: IBR Framework · Find the interest beneath the position · Structure the conversation
3
Zone 1 — Win-Win Still Possible · Green Zone
Actions Replace Words
Parties begin acting against each other rather than talking through differences. Behavior changes: withdrawal, work-to-rule, passive resistance, pointed public behavior. The conflict is now visible to the crew without being formally named. This is the last level where collaborative resolution is straightforwardly available. Many supervisors first notice the conflict at this level — which means they have already missed Levels 1 and 2.
Tool: Structured mediation · IBR with supervisor facilitation · Clear standard-setting
4
Zone 2 — Win-Lose Territory · Yellow Zone
Images and Coalitions
Parties begin recruiting allies. The conflict takes on narrative form — each party has a story about who the other person is, and that story is being spread to the crew. Coalition formation begins. The crew is being asked to take sides. The conflict is no longer between two people — it is becoming a crew culture problem. Win-win becomes increasingly difficult from here.
Tool: Formal supervisory intervention required · Chain of command notification · Individual conversations with both parties separately
5
Zone 2 — Win-Lose Territory · Yellow Zone
Loss of Face
The conflict has become about dignity and identity, not just the original issue. One or both parties has been humiliated, publicly discredited, or formally shamed. Winning the original issue is no longer the goal — restoring face is. This is where collaborative dialogue becomes counterproductive: the rational conversation assumes both parties are engaged with the problem. At Level 5, both parties are engaged with their survival.
Tool: Structured mediation with neutral third party · Formal documentation · Leadership intervention at supervisory chain level
6
Zone 2 — Win-Lose Territory · Yellow Zone
Threat Strategies
Open threats, ultimatums, demands backed by explicit consequences. Formal complaints, union grievances, HR referrals, EEO filings. The tools of the formal system are being deployed as weapons. The conflict is now partially in institutional channels. The supervisor who has been avoiding the conversation since Level 1 now discovers the full cost of that avoidance. Level 6 was almost always a Level 1 or 2 that wasn't addressed.
Tool: Formal process only · HR/labor relations involvement · Documentation of everything
7
Zone 3 — Lose-Lose Territory · Red Zone
Limited Destruction
Each party is willing to accept harm to themselves if it harms the other party more. The calculation has shifted from "what can I win?" to "how much can I cost them?" Organizational damage is now occurring regardless of outcome. One or both parties may need to be separated permanently.
Tool: Executive leadership · Arbitration · Potential separation · Legal counsel
8–9
Zone 3 — Lose-Lose Territory · Red Zone
Fragmentation and Mutual Destruction
The goal has become the destruction of the other party. Both parties are willing to destroy themselves to achieve it. The organization is absorbing catastrophic damage. At Level 9, Glasl describes it as "together into the abyss." These are the LODDs, the departmental scandals, the careers ended, the lawsuits, the news stories. They almost always trace back to a Level 1 or 2 that no one addressed.
Tool: Legal process only · Criminal referral in extreme cases · Organizational restructuring

The Company Officer's Intervention Window — Levels 1 Through 3

The most important insight in the Glasl framework for fire service company officers: your window for direct, informal, relationship-preserving intervention is Levels 1 through 3. Level 4 requires formal supervisory involvement. Level 5 requires chain of command engagement. Level 6 requires institutional process. Levels 7-9 are largely out of the company officer's authority to resolve.

The reason most fire service conflict reaches Level 5 or 6 before it receives genuine supervisory attention is the conflict avoidance mental model from Module A3 — the belief that "conflict should be avoided on shift" produces a supervisor who notices a Level 1 hardening of positions and actively avoids addressing it. By Level 3, the conflict is visible to the crew but the supervisor is still managing it by not naming it. By Level 4, the crew has formed sides and the damage to the invisible culture is already compounding. By Level 5, the relationship is so damaged that the early, direct, informal conversation that would have resolved it at Level 1 is no longer available.

The most valuable conflict resolution skill a company officer develops is not how to resolve Level 5 conflicts. It is the willingness and ability to address Level 1 conflicts directly, immediately, and without waiting for the situation to become undeniable.

The 5 Whys Applied to Escalation
Before determining the intervention, run the 5 Whys backward from the current escalation level. Level 5 loss-of-face conflict → Why did it reach this level? → Level 3 actions-replace-words visible to the crew for months → Why wasn't it addressed there? → Level 2 debates were happening → Why weren't they addressed? → Level 1 hardening of positions → Why wasn't that addressed? → The supervisor had the conflict avoidance mental model and the crew culture had no psychological safety for surfacing concerns. The 5 Whys almost always reveals that the structural cause of serious conflict is a Level 1 or 2 that was not addressed — and often reveals a supervisor behavior or system condition that produced the original hardening of positions.

This is a Level 4-5 situation. The 2/5 answer says "I would sit them down together and facilitate a conversation." Collaborative dialogue is the right tool for Level 2, not Level 5.

The mistake most supervisors make is waiting until the conflict is undeniable — which usually means it has reached Level 4 or 5, and the most effective interventions are no longer available."

The Thomas-Kilmann Five Modes:
Know Your Default, Choose Your Response

⏱ 24 min
🎯 TKI · Five Conflict Styles · Assertiveness vs. Cooperation · Fire Service Default Modes

Kenneth Thomas and Ralph Kilmann published the Conflict Mode Instrument in 1974. It remains the most widely used conflict style assessment in organizational psychology globally — validated across cultures, industries, and organizational levels for five decades. Its organizing insight is simple and extraordinarily useful: people handle conflict through five distinct modes, and each mode is defined by its position on two axes — how assertive you are in pursuing your own concerns, and how cooperative you are in accommodating the other party's concerns.

The modes are not personality types — they are strategies. The same person can and should use all five modes at different times. The problem is that most people have one or two modes they default to regardless of whether those modes are appropriate for the situation at hand. In the fire service specifically, two default modes are so common they deserve specific attention: Competing (driven by operational culture) and Avoiding (driven by the conflict avoidance mental model). Neither is inherently wrong. Both become significant leadership liabilities when they are applied by default rather than by deliberate choice.

The Five Modes — Complete Assessment

High Assertiveness · Low Cooperation
Competing
Win-lose orientation. You pursue your concerns at the other party's expense. Direct, firm, unilateral. Does not seek the other party's input on the solution.
Right when: Safety is at immediate risk. An unpopular but necessary decision must be made quickly. You are right and you know it and delay causes harm. You need to protect the crew or the community from a clear and present risk.
❌ Misused when: Applied by default to all conflict. Produces resentment, power games, and the suppression of information that the authority gradient creates. The most common fire service default mode — because operational culture rewards decisiveness.
High Assertiveness · High Cooperation
Collaborating
Win-win orientation. You work with the other party to find a solution that fully addresses both parties' concerns. Requires genuine investment in understanding what the other party needs and why. Time-intensive.
Right when: The issue is important enough to warrant the time investment. Both parties' concerns are genuinely legitimate. A creative solution can produce a better outcome than either party's initial position. The relationship is important enough to preserve fully.
❌ Misused when: Applied to trivial issues or when time is genuinely not available. Collaborating on a safety emergency while the building is on fire is not appropriate. But Collaborating on a persistent crew culture conflict is almost always the right mode once the situation is stabilized.
Medium Assertiveness · Medium Cooperation
Compromising
Partial win for both parties. Each party gives up something and gains something. Neither party gets their full preferred outcome, but both get enough to move forward. Faster than Collaborating, more relationship-preserving than Competing.
Right when: The issue is moderately important. Time is limited. A temporary resolution is needed while the deeper issue is addressed. Both parties are equally powerful and equally committed to their position. The relationship must be preserved and full Collaboration is not possible.
❌ Misused when: Applied to issues where one party is clearly right and one is clearly wrong. "Splitting the difference" on a safety standard is not a compromise — it is creating a sub-standard. On core standards, Competing is the right mode.
Low Assertiveness · Low Cooperation
Avoiding
Withdrawal, postponement, or diplomatic sidestepping. The issue is not addressed. Neither party's concerns are actively addressed in the immediate term. Can look like diplomacy but is often conflict avoidance wearing a professional costume.
Right when: The issue is genuinely trivial. The timing is wrong and delay allows emotions to cool before a productive conversation is possible. You need time to gather information before addressing the issue. Addressing it right now would make things worse, not better.
❌ Misused when: Applied as the default to any uncomfortable conflict. The conflict avoidance mental model from Module A3 produces Avoiding as the default mode — which compounds every Level 1 conflict into a Level 4 by the time anyone notices. Lencioni's Dysfunction 2 (Fear of Conflict) is systematic Avoiding.
Low Assertiveness · High Cooperation · Full Row
Accommodating
You yield to the other party's concerns at the expense of your own. The other party's preferences determine the outcome. Can feel like kindness or generosity, but when used systemically produces the artificial harmony failure that Module A4's Lencioni analysis describes.
Right when: You realize you are wrong — genuinely. The other party's concerns are more important in this specific situation. Preserving the relationship is worth more than winning this particular issue. You want to develop the other person's confidence and judgment by letting them lead on this decision.
❌ Misused when: Applied chronically to avoid conflict, displeasure, or the discomfort of holding a standard someone disagrees with. The supervisor who always Accommodates has no standards — only preferences — and the crew knows it. Chronic Accommodating is the leadership mode that produces the crew that no longer believes the standards are real.

The Fire Service Default Mode Problem — Why This Module Exists

Research on fire service leadership consistently finds two dominant default conflict modes: Competing and Avoiding. Neither is inherently problematic. Both become leadership liabilities when applied by default.

The Competing Default comes from operational culture. Decisive, directive, command-and-control leadership is appropriate and effective in tactical emergency management. The same mode applied to interpersonal conflict, personnel management, and crew development produces the authority-based relationship that depletes the Bank of Leadership, creates the authority gradient that NIOSH identifies in LODD analysis, and generates the formal grievances that Level 6 conflict produces. The company officer who handles a crew member's performance concern the same way they handle an incident command briefing — directive, fast, closed to input — is applying the right mode in the wrong context.

The Avoiding Default comes from the conflict avoidance mental model and the cultural norm that conflict threatens crew harmony. The company officer who noticed the Level 1 hardening of positions in December and has been carefully not addressing it since is Avoiding. By March it is a Level 4 coalition situation visible to the entire crew. The avoidance felt like kindness. It was negligence toward the invisible culture the entire crew inhabits.

The Mode Selection Protocol — Three Questions Before You Respond
1. What is the escalation level? (From Lesson 1) Green zone → Collaborating or Compromising. Yellow zone → Competing for immediate stabilization, then structured mediation. Red zone → formal process only.

2. How important is the issue to the mission, to safety, or to the crew's culture? High importance + non-negotiable standard → Competing. High importance + legitimate divergence of interests → Collaborating. Moderate importance + relationship preservation → Compromising.

3. How important is the relationship? Long-term crew relationship requiring genuine investment → Collaborating. Short-term situational conflict → Competing or Compromising. The cost of maintaining the relationship must be weighed against the cost of the issue going unresolved.
Oral Board — TKI Mode Questions
Layer 2
"A senior crew member with 15 years on the job consistently challenges your decisions in briefings — loudly, in front of the crew. How do you handle this?"
Mode selection is the core skill here. Avoiding (the temptation) produces a crew culture where authority is openly negotiable. Competing publicly (the other temptation) escalates the conflict and models exactly the behavior you're trying to stop. The 4/5 answer Avoids the public arena, then uses Competing privately to set the standard clearly, then shifts to Collaborating to understand what is driving the behavior. Three modes, in sequence, for the same situation.
Layer 1
"What is your natural conflict style — and how do you know when it is the wrong style for the situation you are in?"
The TKI self-awareness question. The 5/5 answer names a specific default mode, gives a specific example of when it served them well, and gives a specific example of when they recognized it was the wrong mode and shifted. The ability to self-assess in real time is the most sophisticated conflict skill available and the rarest one assessors encounter.
Lesson 2 — The Five Conflict Modes
Five modes: Competing (high assertiveness, low cooperation), Collaborating (high-high), Compromising (medium-medium), Avoiding (low-low), Accommodating (low assertiveness, high cooperation). All five are legitimate when matched to the right situation. All five are liabilities when applied by default regardless of situation.
The fire service default problem: Competing (from operational culture) applied to interpersonal conflict produces the authority gradient. Avoiding (from the conflict avoidance mental model) applied to early conflict produces the Level 4-5 situations that formal process must then manage.
The mode selection protocol: read the escalation level, assess the importance of the issue, assess the importance of the relationship. The intersection of those three answers produces the right mode. Apply it intentionally, not by default.
The Five Modes at a Glance
Competing
Win-lose. Right for safety/urgency. Misused when default in all conflict.
Collaborating
Win-win. Right for important issues with legitimate divergence. Best long-term mode.
Compromising
Partial win both sides. Right when time-constrained and relationship matters.
Avoiding
Withdraw or postpone. Right when timing is wrong. Misused when it's just fear.
Accommodating
Yield to other party. Right when you're wrong. Misused chronically = no standards.
Three-Question Mode Selection
Escalation level? Importance of issue? Importance of relationship? The answers produce the mode. Apply intentionally, not by default.

Interests vs. Positions:
The Framework That Unlocks Every Conflict

⏱ 24 min
🔑 Fisher & Ury · Getting to Yes · Harvard Negotiation Project · The IBR Framework

In 1981, Roger Fisher and William Ury at the Harvard Negotiation Project published Getting to Yes — one of the most influential conflict resolution texts in the history of organizational psychology. Its central insight has survived four decades of research, replication, and application across virtually every conflict domain imaginable: positions are incompatible. Interests almost always are not.

A position is what someone says they want. An interest is why they want it. The position is the stated demand. The interest is the need, concern, fear, or aspiration that produced the demand. When two parties negotiate between their positions, they are arguing over incompatible outcomes in a zero-sum game — one party's gain is the other's loss. When two parties explore their underlying interests, they almost always discover that the interests are compatible, complementary, or at minimum not mutually exclusive — and that there are multiple solutions that could satisfy both parties' interests that neither party thought of because they were too busy arguing over their positions.

The IBR Framework — People, Interests, Options, Criteria

P
People — Separate the Person from the Problem
Treat every party as a person with legitimate needs, not just as an opposing position holder. Attack the problem together. Don't attack the person. This is the EQ Social Awareness dimension from Module A2: the leader who can maintain genuine empathy for both parties while facilitating the conflict has access to information and trust that the leader who takes sides immediately does not. The most common failure at this step: the supervisor who conducts an investigation by first asking Party A what happened, then believing Party A's account, then conducting the "investigation" of Party B as a confirmation exercise. That is confirmation bias (Module 10 Lesson 1 Bias Checklist) masquerading as due process.
I
Interests — Find What's Actually Driving the Conflict
"I want him transferred" is a position. "I need the shift dynamic to be safe and functional so I can do my job effectively" is an interest. "I want her disciplined" is a position. "I need the crew to understand that the standard applies to everyone equally" is an interest. The position closes options. The interest opens them. The diagnostic question that surfaces interests: "Tell me what you need this situation to look like in six months — not what you need to happen to him, but what you need your shift experience to look like." That question cannot be answered with a position. It requires the person to articulate an interest — and the interest almost always has overlap with the other party's interest.
O
Options — Generate Before Evaluating
Before evaluating any solution, generate multiple possible solutions. Most fire service conflict resolution jumps to the first viable option — which is almost never the best one. The IBR discipline: generate at least four possible solutions to the interests identified, without evaluating any of them until the list is complete. This is brainstorming applied to conflict resolution, and it consistently produces solutions that neither party had considered and that satisfy both parties' interests better than the position either party originally advocated for. Schedule changes, role assignments, structured communication protocols, mediated agreements — the options are almost always broader than the original competing positions suggested.
C
Criteria — Agree on Standards Before Evaluating Options
Agree on objective criteria for evaluating the generated options before evaluating any of them. What makes a good solution here? Safety of the crew? Operational continuity? Fairness as measured by consistent application of policy? Relationship preservation? The person's dignity? Once both parties agree on the criteria — before any option is evaluated — the evaluation process becomes grounded in shared standards rather than competing preferences. The party whose preferred option fails the agreed criteria cannot simply advocate harder for it. The criteria do the work of preventing the negotiation from reverting to a positional argument.

"Positions are what people say they want. Interests are why they want it. Positions are incompatible. Interests almost always are not. The entire conflict resolution discipline begins with learning to see behind the position to the interest that produced it."

— After Fisher & Ury · Getting to Yes · Harvard Negotiation Project · Module 11

Position vs. Interest — A Complete Fire Service Walk-Through

The situation: FF Martinez has told her company officer that she will not work with FF Thompson under any circumstances and is requesting a permanent schedule change to avoid being on shift with him. FF Thompson says the same about Martinez.

Position-level analysis: Two incompatible positions. One of them can be scheduled differently or neither can — but neither position directly addresses the operational or relational problem that produced them. Any "solution" at the position level leaves the underlying interests unaddressed and guarantees recurrence.

Interest-level inquiry: The company officer conducts separate conversations with each crew member. To Martinez: "Tell me what you need your shift experience to look like — not what needs to happen to Thompson, but what you need." Martinez's interest: to feel safe from the behavior she experienced as disrespectful without having to choose between her dignity and her schedule. To Thompson: "What do you need this situation to look like in six months?" Thompson's interest: to be heard about what he perceived as Martinez's unfair characterization of his behavior, and to have a clear understanding of what the standard actually is going forward.

The interest overlap: Both parties want clarity about the standard, dignity in their work environment, and operational functionality. Those interests are compatible. The IBR process now generates options that address those interests — which may include a structured mediated conversation with the officer present, a clear documented agreement about communication standards, a period of supervisor-facilitated interaction with specific behavioral expectations, and a follow-up timeline. None of those options were available at the position level.

Role Play

IBR answer: Before any schedule decision, the officer needs to understand the interest beneath the position. "Tell me what happened and what you need this situation to look like going forward — not what needs to happen to your partner, but what you need your shift experience to look like." That one question signals that you are not going to simply negotiate between positions — you are going to understand what actually needs to be addressed. The 4/5 response does this before making any commitment about the schedule.

The CALM Model & DESC Script:
The Practical Toolkit

⏱ 22 min
🛠️ CALM · DESC · Conversation Structure · Assessment-Day Tools · The Two-Framework System

The escalation ladder tells you what level you are dealing with. The TKI five modes tell you which approach to take. The IBR framework tells you to find the interest beneath the position. Now you need the actual words — the specific conversational structure that makes all of that framework knowledge operational in real time, under assessment-day pressure, in a role play exercise where you have three minutes to demonstrate conflict resolution competency.

This lesson gives you two complementary frameworks. The CALM model is the sequence for conducting a conflict resolution conversation from start to finish. The DESC script is the structure for assertively initiating a conflict conversation — specifically for the moment when you need to name a problem clearly, firmly, and without aggression. Together, they give you a complete conversation architecture: DESC opens the conversation when you are the one addressing a performance or behavior issue, and CALM guides the full resolution process regardless of who initiated it.

The CALM Model — The Four-Step Conflict Resolution Sequence

The CALM model is the most immediately deployable framework in this module — four steps, memorable under pressure, sophisticated enough to produce a genuine 4/5 response in any role play scenario. It operationalizes everything in Lessons 1 through 3 into a practical conversation sequence.

C
Step 1
Clarify — Understand Before Concluding
Understand the conflict fully from all perspectives before forming any judgment, reaching any conclusion, or deciding on any response. Ask more questions than you answer. This is the moment when most supervisors fail: they have already concluded who is right and who is wrong before they have spoken to both parties, because the first party to present their case has activated confirmation bias (Module 10 Bias Checklist). Clarify is the deliberate interruption of that automatic process. "Tell me what happened from your perspective" — and then genuinely listen without simultaneously building your response. Clarify applies to both parties, separately, before you form any view about the situation.
A
Step 2
Address — The Behavior, Not the Person
Address the specific behavior or situation that is creating the conflict — not the person's character, intentions, or worth as a crew member. This is the SBI structure from Module 10 Lesson 6 in direct application: Situation (when and where), Behavior (what you observed that a camera would have captured), Impact (the specific consequence on the crew, the operation, or the individual). "You have a communication problem" is a character assessment. "In Tuesday's briefing, when you interrupted FF Martinez twice while she was speaking, she stopped contributing for the rest of the shift and two other crew members pulled back" is a specific, observable, SBI-structured behavioral address. The second version can be responded to. The first can only be accepted or defended against.
L
Step 3
Listen — Genuinely, Not Performatively
Active, genuine listening — not waiting to speak, not preparing your rebuttal while they are still talking, not communicating through your body language that you have already decided. Reflect back what you heard: "What I'm hearing is that you felt the standard wasn't applied consistently, and that's what produced your response. Is that accurate?" Name the emotion beneath the words: "It sounds like this situation has felt unfair to you — not just frustrating, but unfair. Am I reading that right?" This is Domain 3 EQ (Social Awareness) from Module A2 in direct application. The leader who genuinely listens gets information that changes the intervention. The one who performs listening misses the interest that would have unlocked the resolution.
M
Step 4
Manage — Toward Resolution and Follow-Through
Manage toward a resolution that addresses the underlying interest, not just the presenting position. This is where IBR (Lesson 3) activates: generate options that address what both parties actually need, apply agreed criteria to evaluate those options, and reach a resolution that is specific enough to be followed through and verified. Then follow through. The most consistent failure in fire service conflict resolution is the conversation that ends with a good resolution and no follow-up — which signals to the crew that the conversation was a performance rather than a genuine commitment. "I'll check in with both of you in two weeks about how this is going" and then actually checking in is the difference between a 3/5 and a 4/5 resolution. Documentation belongs here when the situation warrants it.

The DESC Script — Assertive Conflict Initiation

CALM is the resolution framework. DESC is the assertion framework — specifically designed for the moment when you are the one who needs to initiate a conflict conversation about a behavior or performance issue. Sharon and Gordon Bower developed DESC in their conflict assertion research, and it solves a specific problem: how do you raise a difficult issue directly, specifically, and firmly without being aggressive, without being vague, and without leaving the other person confused about what you need from them?

DESC also pairs directly with SBI (Module 10 Lesson 6): DESC provides the overall conversation structure, and the SBI model provides the specific language for the D (Describe) and E (Express) steps. Together they give you a complete conversation from opening to commitment.

D
Describe — Observable Fact, Not Interpretation
State specifically what you observed
Describe the specific situation or behavior objectively — what a video camera would have captured. This is the SBI Situation + Behavior step. Observable fact only, no interpretation, no character assessment, no history.
"FF Rodriguez, in the last three shift briefings — on Tuesday, Thursday, and today — the apparatus check has not been completed before the briefing time."
E
Express — How It Affects the Crew or Mission
State the specific impact on operations, safety, or culture
Express how the situation affects the crew, the operation, or the mission — not how it makes you feel about Rodriguez as a person. This is the SBI Impact step. Specific, operational, not personal.
"When the apparatus check isn't complete before briefing, the oncoming crew doesn't have accurate information about the unit's status, which is a safety issue and a crew reliability issue."
S
Specify — Exactly What You Need Changed
Name the specific behavioral change required
Specify exactly what you need to happen differently — specific enough that both parties have the same understanding of what "compliance" looks like. Vague standards produce vague compliance. Specific behavioral standards produce specific behavioral change.
"I need the apparatus check completed and the log signed before 0800 briefing, starting today. That's the standard for this crew."
C
Consequences — Both Positive and Negative
State what happens if the standard is met and if it isn't
State both consequences: the positive consequence of compliance (what continues, improves, or becomes possible) and the negative consequence of non-compliance (what formal action follows). Both consequences should be specific and credible. This is not a threat — it is transparency about what the situation requires.
"If this is consistent going forward, we won't need to have this conversation again and I won't need to document it. If the pattern continues, I will need to start formal documentation and involve the chain of command. I'd strongly prefer the first outcome."
DESC + CALM Together — The Complete Conversation Architecture
In practice, DESC initiates the conversation (Describe the behavior, Express the impact, Specify what's needed, state the Consequences). Then CALM guides the full resolution process: Clarify (listen to their perspective — sometimes the DESC conversation reveals new information that changes the intervention), Address (using SBI if additional behavioral specificity is needed), Listen (genuinely, for the interest beneath their response), Manage (toward a specific resolution with follow-through built in). The two frameworks are not competing — DESC opens the door and CALM takes you through it.
Oral Board — CALM & DESC Application
Role Play
"A crew member has been late to shift briefings three times this week. Walk me through how you would handle this conversation."
DESC answer: "I would start by describing the specific observable behavior — 'You've arrived after the 0800 briefing start time on Monday, Wednesday, and Thursday this week.' Then express the specific impact — 'When that happens, we either delay the briefing for the whole crew or start without you and you miss critical shift information.' Then specify what I need — 'I need you at the station and ready for briefing at 0800, every shift.' Then state both consequences clearly. Then shift to CALM — Clarify by asking if there's something I should know about, Listen genuinely to the response."
Layer 1
"How do you approach a conflict conversation when the crew member becomes emotional or defensive during the conversation?"
The CALM "Listen" step is the answer. When a crew member becomes emotional, the first move is to slow down, genuinely reflect back what you're hearing, and name the emotion beneath it without judgment. "I can see this is frustrating for you" — and then wait. The defensive response almost always contains the interest that will unlock the resolution, if you listen for it instead of responding to the defensiveness.
Lesson 4 — CALM & DESC
CALM (Clarify, Address, Listen, Manage) is the four-step conflict resolution conversation sequence. It operationalizes Lessons 1-3 into a specific conversation structure. Most role play exercises can be fully addressed with CALM deployed competently.
DESC (Describe, Express, Specify, Consequences) is the assertive conflict initiation structure — for when you are the one raising the issue. Specific behavior + specific impact + specific standard + specific consequences. Vague DESC produces vague compliance.
DESC opens, CALM guides. Together they produce a complete conversation architecture: DESC establishes what the issue is and what needs to change; CALM ensures the conversation is mutual, genuinely heard, and resolved toward a specific outcome with follow-through built in.
The CALM Model
C — Clarify
Understand from all perspectives before concluding. Interrupt confirmation bias. Both parties, separately, first.
A — Address
The behavior, not the person. SBI structure: Situation, Behavior, Impact. Observable only.
L — Listen
Genuinely. Reflect back. Name the emotion. This is where the interest lives.
M — Manage
Toward resolution of the interest. Specific outcome. Follow-through built in. Document when warranted.
The DESC Script
D — Describe
Observable fact. Camera-level specificity. No interpretation.
E — Express
Specific operational impact on crew, mission, or safety.
S — Specify
Exactly what changes. Specific enough that compliance is measurable.
C — Consequences
Both outcomes. Positive for compliance. Negative for non-compliance. Specific and credible.

Crucial Conversations:
When the Stakes Are High and the Emotions Are Real

⏱ 24 min
💬 Patterson & Grenny · VitalSmarts · STATE Skills · Silence vs. Violence · The Pool of Meaning

Kerry Patterson, Joseph Grenny, Ron McMillan, and Al Switzler published Crucial Conversations in 2002 — one of the most widely applied organizational communication frameworks in the two decades since. Its organizing definition is precise and useful: a crucial conversation is any conversation where the stakes are high, opinions differ, and emotions run strong. These three factors together produce the conditions under which most people do exactly the wrong thing — they either go silent (avoid the issue entirely) or go violent (attack, dominate, or coerce).

The framework is specifically designed for the hardest version of the conflict resolution problem — not the everyday performance conversation (which DESC and CALM handle well) but the conversation where the emotional charge is high enough that the standard frameworks tend to collapse under the pressure. The crucial conversation about a crew member whose performance problem has become a safety issue. The conversation with the BC whose decision you genuinely believe is wrong. The conversation with the crew member who has been formally accused of workplace harassment. The debrief of a near-fatal operational incident where decisions were made that should not have been.

The Two Default Failures — Silence and Violence

When the stakes are high, the amygdala activates the threat response (System 1, from Module A2), and most people default to one of two failure modes:

Silence: Withdrawal, avoidance, masking genuine concerns with surface agreement, changing the subject, or leaving the conversation entirely before it is resolved. Silence feels safe in the moment and is catastrophic over time — it is the conflict avoidance mental model (A3) in real-time action. The crucial conversation that ends in silence leaves the issue unresolved, the relationship damaged, and the situation worse than before the conversation was attempted.

Violence: Controlling, labeling, attacking. Not necessarily physical — in organizational settings, violence is the conversation where one party dominates through force of personality, rank, anger, or the overwhelming volume of their certainty. Violence produces compliance in the short term and resentment, distancing, and information suppression in the long term. The supervisor who wins every crucial conversation through dominance eventually leads a crew that tells them only what is safe to say.

The crucial conversation framework creates a third path between Silence and Violence: a conversation structure that maintains enough psychological safety for both parties to engage genuinely with a genuinely difficult issue.

Creating the Pool of Shared Meaning

Patterson and Grenny describe the goal of a crucial conversation as building a "pool of shared meaning" — a shared, accurate, complete understanding of the situation, including each party's perspective, concerns, and needs, that both parties genuinely contribute to and both parties genuinely believe is accurate. The pool of shared meaning is what makes good decisions possible — decisions based on the full picture rather than on each party's partial, privately held version of events.

The pool is built through the STATE skills — and the most important thing to understand about the STATE sequence is the order. Most people start at step T (Tell your story) and skip step S (Share your facts), which immediately puts the other party on the defensive because they are receiving an interpretation, not a fact, and they know the difference even if they cannot always name it.

The STATE Skills — The Crucial Conversation Framework

S
Share Your Facts First
Start with observable data, not interpretation
Share the observable facts — what you actually saw, heard, or measured — before sharing any interpretation of those facts. "In the last four shifts, the apparatus check has been unsigned at shift change" is a fact. "You've been slacking on your responsibilities and it's affecting the crew" is an interpretation. The fact can be verified and corrected if wrong. The interpretation produces defensiveness immediately because it is a character assessment delivered as fact. Most crucial conversations fail at this step — because the person initiating the conversation leads with their story (interpretation) and the other party's first response is to defend themselves rather than to engage with the issue.
T
Tell Your Story — As a Story, Not as Truth
Share your interpretation — but present it as an interpretation
Once you have shared the observable facts, share the story you made from those facts — but present it explicitly as your interpretation, not as established truth. "I've been wondering if something has changed for you on shift, or whether there's something about the apparatus check process that isn't working — because the pattern is new and I want to understand it before I draw any conclusions." This is the Ladder of Inference (Module A3) applied in real time: you are naming your current position on the ladder and explicitly acknowledging that you are still at the interpretation step, not at the conclusion step.
A
Ask for Their Path
Genuinely invite their perspective — and mean it
Ask sincerely about the other person's perspective, feelings, and story. "Help me understand how you see this" or "What am I missing here?" — and then actually listen for what you are missing, rather than listening for confirmation of what you already concluded. This is the interest-surfacing practice from Lesson 3 embedded in the crucial conversation sequence. The person's "path" — how they got to their current position — almost always contains information that changes the picture, and sometimes changes it enough that the conversation's direction needs to update as a result.
T
Talk Tentatively
Present conclusions with appropriate uncertainty
Share your conclusions and interpretations with appropriate tentativeness — not weakness, but epistemic honesty. "I'm wondering if..." rather than "You obviously..." "It seems to me..." rather than "The fact is..." "I may be wrong about this, but the pattern I'm seeing suggests..." This is not hedging. It is the difference between presenting an interpretation as if it were a fact (which produces defensiveness and conflict) and presenting it as what it actually is (which produces the space for the other person to add their perspective without having to "correct" you). Talking tentatively makes genuine dialogue possible. Talking with false certainty makes dialogue impossible and argument inevitable.
E
Encourage Testing
Make it explicitly safe to disagree, add, or push back
Explicitly make it safe for the other person to disagree with your view, add to it, or offer a completely different perspective. "I want to know if I'm seeing this wrong" — and mean it. "If you see this differently, I genuinely want to hear that." This is the challenger safety practice from Module A4 applied at the conversation level: the leader who explicitly invites challenge and genuinely receives it when it comes is modeling the psychological safety that makes crucial conversations possible in a crew culture. The leader who says "feel free to push back" and then becomes defensive when someone does has signaled that challenging is only safe in theory, not in practice.
Oral Board — Crucial Conversations Questions
Layer 2
"Tell me about the most difficult conversation you've had as a supervisor — one where the stakes were high, you genuinely weren't sure how it would go, and the other person was emotional. Walk me through how you prepared and how the conversation actually went."
This is the crucial conversation question. The 5/5 answer describes specific preparation (what facts were gathered, what story the candidate was aware they were bringing, what they did to create safety at the start of the conversation), specific things said and why, and what they would do differently. Generic "I stayed calm and had an honest conversation" scores at 2/5. Specific language, specific sequence, specific outcome with reflection scores at 5/5.
Layer 1
"When a conversation with a crew member is going badly — they're becoming defensive, shutting down, or escalating — what specifically do you do?"
The crucial conversations answer: check whether safety has been lost (silence or violence from the other party signals safety is gone), step out of the content of the conversation momentarily to restore safety ("I want to make sure I'm not making you feel attacked — that's not my intent here"), then return to the content once safety is restored. Continuing to push the content when safety is lost never produces the pool of shared meaning. It produces a win in the conversation and a loss in the relationship.

Assessment Center Mastery:
Every Conflict Scenario Solved

This lesson is the synthesis — everything from Lessons 1 through 5 applied to the six conflict scenario types that appear in fire and EMS promotional assessment centers. By the end of this lesson, you will know exactly what to do with any conflict scenario you encounter in a role play, oral board, or in-basket exercise, and you will know specifically what behaviors assessors are scoring.

What Assessors Are Actually Scoring — The Seven Behaviors

Before the scenarios, understand the scoring criteria. Assessors evaluating conflict resolution competency in role play and oral board exercises are looking for these seven specific behaviors. Most candidates demonstrate two or three. Candidates who demonstrate all seven score at the top of the distribution.

  1. Investigates before concluding. Does the candidate gather information from all relevant parties before forming a judgment? Or do they begin with a conclusion and seek confirmation? The diagnostic-before-prescriptive principle that runs through TPS Modules 10, 11, A2, and A3 is the foundation behavior.
  2. Maintains composure under emotional pressure. When the role player becomes emotional, defensive, or aggressive — does the candidate's EQ self-management (A2 Domain 2) hold? Or do they either capitulate (Accommodating mode) or escalate (Competing mode)?
  3. Separates the person from the problem. IBR principle P — does the candidate address the behavior and the situation, or do they make the conflict about the person's character, intentions, or worth?
  4. Finds the interest beneath the position. Does the candidate ask questions that surface what the party actually needs — or do they only respond to the stated demand (position)?
  5. Matches the intervention to the escalation level. Is the collaborative dialogue approach appropriate for the escalation level presented? Or is the candidate applying a Level 2 tool to a Level 5 situation, or a formal process to a Level 2 situation?
  6. Knows when to involve the chain of command. Does the candidate recognize which situations are within their authority to resolve and which require escalation? Most role play scenarios include at least one element that should be escalated — the candidate who misses it is demonstrating a gap in supervisory judgment.
  7. Follows up and closes the loop. Does the candidate commit to a specific follow-up action — not just "I'll check in on this" but "I'll follow up with both of you in two weeks and I want to document what we've agreed to today"? Follow-through and documentation are the behaviors that distinguish a 4/5 from a 3/5 resolution.

The Six Conflict Scenario Types — Complete Playbook

👥Peer ConflictLevel 1–4
Two crew members cannot work together. Each says the other is the problem.
Escalation read: Most peer conflict in assessment scenarios is Level 2-3 — opinions have hardened, behavior has changed, but formal processes have not yet activated. If coalitions have formed, you're at Level 4.
Mode: Collaborating if Level 2-3, formal intervention if Level 4.
IBR: Separate conversations first. Surface the interest beneath each position — what does each person need their shift experience to look like? The interests are almost always compatible.
Tools: CALM (Clarify both parties separately before addressing) + IBR (interest surfacing) + DESC if a standard has been violated
👑Authority ChallengeLevel 2–3
A crew member challenges your decision publicly or refuses to comply with a directive.
Escalation read: Level 2-3. Win-win still possible but the public element requires immediate mode response.
Mode: Competing in the moment to maintain the standard publicly, then Collaborating privately to understand the concern. Never negotiate the standard publicly — that depletes the Bank of Leadership in front of the entire crew. Address the resistance privately.
Critical: Separate the legitimate concern (which may deserve genuine consideration) from the inappropriate method of raising it (which requires addressing regardless).
Tools: Competing publicly (immediate) + CALM privately (follow-up) + DESC if the behavior recurs
📋Performance ConflictLevel 1–3
A crew member disputes a performance evaluation, a disciplinary action, or a standard applied to them.
Escalation read: Usually Level 1-2 with high emotional charge. The dispute makes it feel like Level 3-4 but the institutional relationship is intact.
Mode: Collaborating — the person has a legitimate right to understand and respond to an evaluation. Their perspective may contain information that is accurate and relevant.
IBR: Listen for the interest. Is this about fairness (consistent application), accuracy (specific behavior description), or process (were they told what was expected)? Each has a different response.
Tools: Crucial Conversations STATE (share facts of the evaluation before telling the story of why it was given) + CALM + IBR for interests
⚖️Values ConflictLevel 2–4 · Complex
A crew member refuses an assignment or a decision on ethical, moral, or values grounds.
Escalation read: Level 2-4 depending on how the refusal has been handled. The most complex scenario type because the person may be right.
Mode: Collaborating first — values conflicts require genuine engagement with the concern, not just authority assertion. But Competing if safety is at risk.
The most important question: Is this an ethics issue (they may be right and the directive may be wrong), a values misalignment (their values are legitimate but the organizational direction takes precedence), or an insubordination issue (they are using values language to resist a legitimate directive)?
IBR critical: Find the interest. What specific harm are they trying to prevent or value are they trying to honor?
Tools: IBR (P — separate the person's legitimate concern from the position of refusal) + Crucial Conversations STATE + chain of command if resolution is beyond your authority
🔄Generational / Cultural ConflictLevel 1–3
Different crew members with incompatible working styles, communication preferences, or cultural backgrounds are creating friction.
Escalation read: Usually Level 1-2 if addressed early; Level 3 if informal coalitions are forming around generational or cultural lines.
Mode: Collaborating — this is a systems issue (the crew culture) as much as an individual conflict. The 5 Whys almost always reveals an invisible norm that one group takes for granted and another group finds exclusionary.
EQ critical: Domain 3 Social Awareness (A2) — the leader who can read the collective emotional temperature of a crew divided by generational or cultural friction can address the invisible culture before it becomes a formal conflict.
Tools: CALM (Clarify the invisible norm, not just the presenting friction) + IBR + EQ Domain 3 social awareness throughout
📑Union / Labor ConflictLevel 4–6
A discipline decision or policy change is being contested by the union. A grievance has been filed or threatened.
Escalation read: Level 4-6. Formal process is already involved. The collaborative dialogue tools of Levels 1-3 are supplementary here, not primary.
Mode: Competing on the formal process (the decision must be defensible procedurally) + Collaborating on the relationship (Marcus the cooperative shop steward from Chiefs Counsel can become an ally or an adversary depending on how the relationship is managed).
Critical behaviors: Follow the contract. Document everything. Brief the chain of command before the conversation. Know which aspects of the decision are within your authority and which require authorization above your level.
Tools: Chiefs Counsel simulation personas (Marcus, Sandra, Tony) + Just Culture branch (was the discipline decision correctly categorized?) + formal process only

The Seven Common Candidate Failures — Avoid These

These are the specific errors assessors score against — behaviors that convert a 4/5 into a 2/5 regardless of how good the candidate's intentions are:

  • Jumping to solution before diagnosis. "Here's what I'd do" before "Tell me what happened." Prescribing before understanding. Every time.
  • Taking sides with the first party. Conducting the "investigation" of Party B as confirmation of Party A's account. Confirmation bias wearing a supervisory costume.
  • Accommodating to avoid discomfort. Giving Party A what they want in their position to end the uncomfortable conversation, without addressing the underlying issue. Produces recurrence.
  • Competing when collaboration is warranted. Applying operational decisiveness to a personnel conversation that requires empathy and inquiry. Operational mode in interpersonal context.
  • Avoiding when direct intervention is required. "I'll monitor the situation" when a direct conversation was required three weeks ago. Level 4 conflict that is still being "monitored."
  • No follow-up or documentation commitment. Ending the conversation without a specific follow-up action, timeline, or documentation commitment. The resolution that exists only in the conversation is not a resolution.
  • Treating the position as the real problem. Negotiating between what each party says they want, without ever asking what they actually need. Position-level resolution of an interest-level conflict.

The Complete Conflict Resolution Sequence — Assessment Day Reference

Step 1 — Read the Escalation Level (Glasl). Green zone (1-3): your direct intervention, collaborative tools. Yellow zone (4-6): formal involvement, chain of command notification. Red zone (7-9): institutional process, above your authority.

Step 2 — Select the Mode (TKI). What does this situation require? Competing (safety, urgency, clear standard violation), Collaborating (important issue, legitimate divergence, long-term relationship), Compromising (time-constrained, relationship preservation), Avoiding (timing wrong, cooling needed), Accommodating (you're wrong, or it genuinely doesn't matter).

Step 3 — Find the Interest (IBR). Separate conversations if peer conflict. Surface the interest with the interest-surfacing question. Generate options that address the interest, not just the position. Agree on criteria before evaluating.

Step 4 — Use the Right Structure (CALM + DESC + STATE). DESC if you are asserting a standard. CALM for the full resolution conversation. STATE if the conversation is a crucial one — high stakes, differing opinions, strong emotions.

Step 5 — Demonstrate the Seven Scoring Behaviors. Investigate, maintain composure, separate person from problem, find the interest, match the intervention to the level, know when to escalate, follow up and document.

Tell me what happened from your perspective, and then tell me what you need this situation to look like going forward."
