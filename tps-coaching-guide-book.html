The TPS Coaching Philosophy

Your foundational beliefs, principles, and identity as a TPS coach. Read this section before every coaching engagement begins.

The One-Sentence Philosophy

The TPS Coaching Commitment

"I coach you to LEAD — not just to pass the promotional assessment center."

Every coaching decision, every session structure, every piece of feedback you deliver should pass through this filter: am I developing a leader, or am I coaching someone to perform in a room for 90 minutes? The distinction matters because assessors are trained to see the difference. A candidate who has genuinely developed their leadership identity performs differently than a candidate who has memorized responses. Assessors can feel it. You know this — you sat on that side of the table for 10 years.

The Five Coaching Principles

Principle 1 — Diagnose Before Prescribe

Never recommend a development intervention without first diagnosing the root cause. Apply the 3 U's to every identified gap. Is the candidate Unaware of what excellence looks like? Unable to produce it yet? Or Unwilling to do the work? The diagnosis determines the prescription — and the wrong prescription wastes precious session time.

In Practice

A candidate who keeps closing without naming a framework is not necessarily Unwilling. They may be Unaware that closing with a named principle is expected and scored. Never discipline an Unaware candidate — educate them first and watch the behavior change immediately.

Principle 2 — Behavior Over Knowledge

Assessment centers score behavior, not knowledge. A candidate can recite every framework in the TPS curriculum and still score 3/5 if they cannot demonstrate the associated behaviors under observation pressure. Your coaching must target observable, scoreable behavior — not content knowledge. The question is never "do they know it?" — it is always "can they do it under pressure?"

Principle 3 — Identity Before Technique

Technique without identity produces inconsistent performance. A candidate who knows STAR-P but has not developed a genuine leadership identity will lose their structure under supplemental questioning pressure. The first session of every engagement — regardless of the candidate's rank or experience — begins with identity work. Who do they believe they are as a leader? What does that identity require of them in the assessment center?

James Clear Architecture

Ask every candidate to complete this sentence in writing before session one: "I am the kind of leader who ___." Their answer tells you more about their development trajectory than any self-rating ever will. A candidate who says "I am the kind of leader who gets results" is outcome-based. A candidate who says "I am the kind of leader who develops the people around me" is identity-based. You are coaching the outcome-based candidates to become identity-based — because that is what produces 5/5 responses.

Principle 4 — The Assessor's Eye

This is your competitive moat. You have sat on assessment panels and written behavioral scores for hundreds of candidates. You know the exact moment a response moves from a 3 to a 4. You know what assessors write when a candidate opens with a framework diagnosis versus when they jump to action. You know the specific language patterns that score well and the ones that score poorly. Deploy this knowledge explicitly — not as hints but as direct coaching: "When you said that, here is what I would have written on the rubric. Here is what you need to say for me to write a 4."

Principle 5 — Honest Over Comfortable

Premium coaching requires honest assessment. You do not soften gap analysis to protect candidate feelings. You name the gap clearly, explain its organizational consequence, and prescribe the specific development work required to close it. Candidates who receive honest assessment and act on it promote. Candidates who receive comfortable feedback repeat their previous score.

The Hard Truth About Comfortable Coaching

The candidate who most needs honest feedback is usually the one who has tested multiple times, knows all the frameworks intellectually, and cannot understand why they keep scoring 3/5. The comfortable answer is "you're almost there, keep practicing." The honest answer is "your identity is still operating at company officer level and your responses reflect it. That is what we are fixing." The honest answer is the one that produces promotion.

Your Coaching Identity Statement

Before you coach anyone else's identity, you need yours. Write this out and keep it visible:

Your Identity as a Coach

"I am a TPS coach who develops fire and EMS professionals into the leaders their departments need them to be — by combining 10 years of assessor experience with genuine investment in each candidate's growth. I coach from both sides of the table. I know what Exceeds Standard looks like because I wrote those scores."

The TPS Coaching Differentiators

  • Assessor experience: You scored candidates. Most coaches were candidates. That distinction is your authority.
  • TPS methodology: A complete, named, coherent framework system — not generic leadership advice.
  • AI-augmented assessment: Your clients receive a comprehensive pre-session analysis before session one that no other coach in fire service prep delivers.
  • Identity-first approach: You develop leaders, not test-takers. The long-term career impact of that distinction is significant.
  • Rank calibration: Your coaching is calibrated to the specific rank being tested — not one-size-fits-all leadership advice.

The Client Engagement Arc

The complete lifecycle of a coaching engagement — from first contact to final session — with protocols for every stage.

Pre-Engagement Protocol

Step 1 — Intake Completion

Send the intake link at tpscoachinglab.com/intake immediately after booking is confirmed. Include this message: "Before our first session I need you to complete a detailed intake form. Please allow 45–60 minutes — the quality of your intake directly determines the depth of your pre-session assessment. Answer every question as if you were in the assessment center — honest, complete, unfiltered."

The most important section is the practice scenario response. This is your single best data point for where they actually are versus where they think they are. A candidate who self-rates 4/5 across the board but writes a 2/5 practice response has a self-awareness gap that needs to be addressed directly in session one.

Step 2 — Generate the AI Assessment

After intake is submitted, go to /coach/candidates/[id] and click Generate AI Assessment. Review all 10 sections carefully before generating the client report. The coach assessment is your clinical document — read it fully. Pay particular attention to Section 9 (Risk Flags) and Section 10 (Coaching Notes) because those sections tell you what to watch for in the relationship, not just what to develop in the candidate.

Step 3 — Generate and Review the Client Report

Generate the client report and download the PDF. Read it as the candidate will read it — not as the coach. Ask yourself: does this document build confidence while naming gaps honestly? Does the closing note sound like something I would genuinely say? If any section feels generic or inaccurate, make coaching notes before session one.

Step 4 — Session One Preparation

Prepare specifically — not generally. Know this candidate's testing history, their primary fear, their stated leadership philosophy, and the three development priorities from the assessment before the session begins. The candidate paid a premium to work with someone who did the analytical work before they arrived. Show them you did.

Standard Session Structure

0–5 min
Opening Check-In
One question: "What has happened since our last session that is relevant to your development?" This surfaces practice completions, work situations, and identity shifts. Do not skip this — it is coaching intelligence.
5–15 min
Homework Review
Did they complete the assignment? If yes — review it with coaching feedback. If no — address why. Non-completion is always data about motivation, workload, or resistance. Do not ignore it.
15–20 min
Session Focus Teaching
Brief framework teaching or coaching concept introduction. Maximum 10 minutes of instruction. This is not a lecture — it is the context for the practice reps that follow.
20–50 min
Live Practice Reps
2–3 oral board scenarios with real-time coaching after each. Use the CLEAR framework to deliver feedback. Target specific behavioral gaps — not general improvement areas. This is the core value of the session.
50–58 min
Progress Observation
Tell the candidate specifically what you observed developing in this session. "Your Action section is now consistently hitting the 50% mark. Your closing principle is still absent — that is our focus before next session."
58–60 min
Homework Assignment
One specific, executable assignment. Not "practice more" — "Run three in-basket scenarios using the Eisenhower Matrix and submit your Loom recordings before Thursday."

Delivering CLEAR Feedback as a Coach

The CLEAR framework works in live coaching sessions exactly as it works in AI feedback — but with one critical addition: the coaching conversation that follows each section.

  • Commend: Name the specific behavior. "You opened with the 3 U's diagnostic and correctly identified this as an Unwilling situation. That is a 4/5 opening."
  • Limitations: Name the gap behaviorally. "Your Action section ended at approximately 35% of your total response time. Assessors were waiting for more specific behavioral actions from you."
  • Examples: Show them what excellence looks like. "Here is what a 5/5 Action section sounds like for this scenario type." Then model it. This is where your assessor experience is irreplaceable.
  • Adjust: Give them one thing to do differently on the next rep. One thing — not three.
  • Repeat: Run the rep again immediately. Development happens in the moment of correction, not in reflection about it.

Closing an Engagement

Session six closes more than a six-week program. It closes a developmental chapter in the candidate's career. Take it seriously. The closing session should include a verbal summary of how far they have come since session one, specific behavioral evidence of their development, an honest assessment of their readiness for their upcoming assessment, and a genuine personal expression of belief in them.

The Closing Statement That Matters

"You came in thinking like a [current rank]. You are leaving thinking like a [target rank]. That is not a technique change — it is an identity change. That will show up in the room."

Specific coaching approaches, exercises, and behavioral indicators for developing each of the four assessment dimensions.

Overview — What Assessors Actually Score

The four dimensions are not categories of knowledge. They are categories of observable behavior. Assessors are trained to watch for specific behavioral indicators and mark them on a structured rubric. Your job as a coach is to make those indicators visible in your candidate's responses — consistently, under pressure, in every scenario type.

Dimension 1 — Supervisory Judgment

What Assessors Are Scoring

Assessors are watching for three things in this order: (1) Does the candidate diagnose before they act? (2) Is the diagnosis correct? (3) Does the action match the diagnosis? A candidate who jumps to action without diagnosis can never score above a 3 regardless of how good their action plan is.

The Coaching Focus

The 3 U's Diagnostic is the highest-value coaching tool for this dimension. Every candidate who scores below 4/5 in Supervisory Judgment is skipping the diagnostic step. Your coaching prompt is always the same: "Stop. Before you tell me what you're going to do — tell me what you think is actually happening and why."

Key Exercise — The Diagnosis-First Drill

Give the candidate a personnel scenario. Before they can say a single action word, they must complete this sentence: "Based on what I know, this is [Unaware/Unable/Unwilling] because ___." Only after the correct diagnosis can they proceed to action. Run this drill 5 times until the diagnostic habit is automatic.

The 3→4 Conversation

Most 3/5 candidates in Supervisory Judgment give technically correct responses — they apply the right policy, notify the right chain of command, document correctly. What they miss is the principled close. Coach them to end every supervisory judgment response with: "Ultimately what guides this decision is [T·C·H·O·E·D·S element] — specifically [how it applies to this scenario]."

Dimension 2 — Interpersonal Skills

What Assessors Are Scoring

This dimension is not about being nice. It is about bias awareness, emotional regulation under interpersonal pressure, and the ability to navigate conflict toward resolution without avoiding the difficulty of the conversation. The candidate who avoids the hard conversation scores a 2. The candidate who has it incorrectly scores a 3. The candidate who has it correctly AND names the framework they are using scores a 4.

The Coaching Focus

Most candidates are decent at interpersonal situations in real life. The assessment center compresses the scenario to 3-5 minutes and applies observation pressure. Your coaching must simulate that pressure. Role play the difficult party. Push back. Be defensive. Be unreasonable. This is where Loom recordings become invaluable — candidates who have never seen themselves managing interpersonal conflict under pressure do not know what assessors see.

Key Exercise — The Bias Check Prompt

After any interpersonal scenario response, ask: "What bias could have affected your response to this scenario?" If they cannot name a bias — Affinity, Confirmation, Recency, or Attribution — they are not demonstrating the self-awareness that separates 3/5 from 4/5 in this dimension.

Dimension 3 — Oral Communication

What Assessors Are Scoring

Structure, time allocation, and closing principle. A response that has perfect content but gives 10% of time to Action scores a 3. A response that has perfect Action weighting but no closing principle scores a 3. Both elements must be present simultaneously to score a 4.

The Coaching Focus

Use a stopwatch. Every practice rep in your sessions gets timed. After each rep tell the candidate their approximate percentage allocation across STAR-P sections. This is not comfortable — but it produces faster development than any other single coaching intervention. When a candidate hears "Your Action section was 22 seconds out of a 4-minute response" they understand the gap immediately in a way they never would from general feedback.

Key Exercise — The Closing Principle Library

Have each candidate develop three personal closing principles — one anchored to T·C·H·O·E·D·S, one to Bank of Leadership, and one to their personal leadership philosophy statement. Practice using the correct closing principle for the correct scenario type until it is automatic.

Dimension 4 — Written Communication

What Assessors Are Scoring

The purpose statement in paragraph one is the single most commonly missed element in written exercises. Assessors are trained to look for it. If it is missing the response cannot score above a 3 regardless of the quality of paragraphs 2-5. Coach this explicitly: "Every written exercise begins with one sentence that tells the reader why this document exists."

The Coaching Focus

Timed practice. Written exercises are always under time pressure. A candidate who writes a perfect Five-Paragraph memo in 45 minutes is not ready for a 30-minute assessment exercise. Your written exercise coaching must always include a timer.

Key Exercise — The 30-Minute Drill

Set a 30-minute timer. Give a written exercise scenario. Stop them at 30 minutes regardless of where they are. Review what they completed. Grade on the elements present, not the elements missing. Over time they learn to prioritize — purpose statement, analysis, recommendation — over background and close. That prioritization is the difference between a timed 3/5 and a timed 4/5.

Coaching Philosophy for All Buckets

Every bucket has a primary framework. That framework is not optional — it is the analytical lens the assessors expect. A 5/5 response names the primary framework explicitly within the first 90 seconds. Your coaching question for every bucket is always the same: "What is the primary framework for this type of scenario and when did you name it?"

The Universal Coaching Protocol for Any Bucket

The Four-Question Sequence

Before every practice rep, ask these four questions in sequence. They train the diagnostic habit that separates 4/5 from 3/5 candidates.

1. "What bucket is this scenario in?" — Tests scenario recognition.
2. "What is the primary framework for this bucket?" — Tests framework recall under pressure.
3. "What is the assessor specifically scoring in this scenario?" — Tests assessor perspective.
4. "What does a 5/5 response to this scenario look like — not sound like, look like behaviorally?" — Tests performance calibration.

The TPS Framework Coaching Guide

How to teach, develop, and verify application of the core TPS frameworks in live coaching sessions.

Framework Coaching Principle

A candidate who can recite a framework definition scores a 3. A candidate who can apply it correctly to a novel scenario scores a 4. A candidate who integrates it with two other frameworks and closes with it as a leadership principle scores a 5. Your coaching progressions: Recite → Apply → Integrate → Internalize.

The Core Supervisory Frameworks

3 U's Diagnostic

How to teach it: Give three identical scenario descriptions — one Unaware version, one Unable version, one Unwilling version. Have the candidate identify which is which. Then ask: "What in the scenario tells you which U this is?" This trains the diagnostic instinct, not just the label recall.

Common misapplication: Candidates correctly identify the U but then recommend the wrong intervention. The most common error is recommending documentation or progressive discipline for an Unaware situation. This scores a 2 in Supervisory Judgment regardless of how well the rest of the response is structured.

Mastery indicator: Candidate opens every Personnel Issue response with an explicit 3 U's diagnosis within the first 30 seconds — automatically, without being prompted.

Skill/Will Matrix

How to teach it: Draw the 2x2 on screen during the Zoom session. Have the candidate place each scenario character in the correct quadrant and defend the placement. Then match the quadrant to the leadership response: delegate, motivate, coach, or structure.

Common misapplication: Treating High Skill/Low Will as a development problem rather than a motivation or root cause investigation. Candidates jump to coaching when they should first investigate why a capable person is choosing not to perform.

Mastery indicator: Candidate names the specific quadrant and its corresponding leadership response in every scenario involving a team member's performance or behavior.

The Core Conflict Frameworks

Glasl Escalation Ladder

How to teach it: Present three conflict scenarios — one clearly Phase 1, one clearly Phase 2, one at the Phase 2/3 boundary. Have the candidate identify the phase, defend the identification with specific behavioral signals from the scenario, and explain what intervention the phase requires. The key teaching point: using Phase 2 tools on Phase 1 conflict escalates it rather than resolving it.

Common misapplication: Jumping to administrative involvement (Phase 2 response) for a scenario where the conflict signals are clearly still in Phase 1. This scores a 3 at best because the intervention doesn't match the diagnosis.

Mastery indicator: Candidate identifies the phase before naming any intervention — every time, automatically.

IBR — Interest-Based Resolution

How to teach it: Role play a conflict where you play a resistant party. When the candidate asks "what do you want?" — push back with a position statement. Coach them to reframe to: "I hear what you want — help me understand what you need." The distinction between positions and interests is the entire mechanism of IBR and most candidates conflate them.

Mastery indicator: Candidate explicitly separates positions from interests in their language: "His position is X — but what I need to understand is what underlying interest is driving that position."

The Core Leadership Philosophy Frameworks

T·C·H·O·E·D·S

How to teach it: Have the candidate memorize the seven elements in order — Trust, Courage, Humility, Ownership, Empathy, Dedication, Service. Then map each element to a specific real situation from their career. "Tell me a time when your Courage as a leader was tested. What did you do and what did it cost you?" This moves T·C·H·O·E·D·S from a memorized list to a lived leadership vocabulary.

Common misapplication: Candidates cite T·C·H·O·E·D·S generically — "I believe in Trust" — without connecting the specific element to the specific scenario. Assessors score the application, not the citation.

Mastery indicator: Candidate names the specific T·C·H·O·E·D·S element most relevant to the scenario and explains why that element specifically is being tested — not T·C·H·O·E·D·S generally.

Bank of Leadership

How to teach it: Have the candidate audit their current leadership account. "What have you deposited in the last 30 days? What have you withdrawn? What is your current balance with your crew?" This makes the Bank of Leadership concrete and personal rather than theoretical. Then connect it to the coaching scenario: "What does your response to this scenario deposit or withdraw from your leadership account?"

Mastery indicator: Candidate uses deposit/withdrawal language naturally in their response close — "How I handle this situation is a direct deposit or withdrawal from the account that allows me to lead effectively under pressure."

The Advanced Frameworks (Tier 2 Clients)

Heifetz Adaptive vs. Technical

Why it matters at BC+ level: Battalion Chief and above scenarios routinely present adaptive challenges dressed as technical problems. A candidate who applies a technical solution to an adaptive challenge demonstrates exactly the kind of thinking that limits organizations. Coaching this framework at BC+ level is one of the highest-value interventions you can make.

How to teach it: Present a chronic organizational problem — low morale, high turnover, culture issues, resistance to change. Ask: "If this were a technical problem, what would the solution be? Now — why has that technical solution not worked?" The realization that the technical solution exists but hasn't fixed the problem is the entry point to understanding adaptive leadership.

Goleman's EQ Domains

How to teach it: Map the four domains to the 3 U's. Self-Awareness deficits often produce Unaware supervisory gaps. Social Awareness deficits produce Interpersonal skills gaps. Relationship Management is the executing layer where all four dimensions converge. This mapping makes EQ coaching practically applicable to assessment center development rather than abstractly developmental.

Framework Integration — The 5/5 Synthesis

The difference between a 4/5 and a 5/5 response is framework synthesis. Here is a live coaching exercise that develops it:

The Framework Integration Challenge

Give the candidate a compound scenario — one that involves a personnel issue with a conflict element and a change management context (these exist in real assessment centers at BC and above). Tell them: "Your response must naturally integrate three named TPS frameworks. You cannot mention a framework without applying it. You cannot apply a framework without naming it. The three frameworks must be connected to each other — not used in isolation."

This exercise produces more development per session than any amount of single-framework practice. It forces the cognitive synthesis that characterizes 5/5 performance.

The High-Stakes Conversation — Four Weeks Out and Not Ready

This is the hardest conversation in coaching. A candidate who is four weeks from their assessment and still not performing at 4/5 level needs to hear something difficult from you. You have two choices and neither is comfortable.

Option A — Honest Assessment

"Based on where we are right now, if your assessment were tomorrow I would assess you as likely to score in the 3/5 range across most dimensions. That may or may not be enough to promote depending on the competitive field. Here is what you can do in four weeks to close the remaining gap — and here is what you cannot close in four weeks. Let's talk about whether to proceed or whether to request a timeline adjustment from your department."

Option B — Maximum Acceleration

"We need to change our approach for the final four weeks. Daily practice sessions — minimum 30 minutes. Every session recorded on Loom and submitted for review. We are going to focus exclusively on the two dimensions where you are most vulnerable. No more general practice — only targeted reps on your gaps. If you are willing to do that I believe you can close the gap. Tell me if you're in."

The choice between these options is yours to make with the candidate based on your honest assessment of their trajectory and their capacity for accelerated development. What is never an option is false reassurance. A candidate who enters an assessment with inflated confidence about their readiness performs worse than a candidate who enters with a clear-eyed understanding of their gaps and a plan to manage them.

Managing the Coaching Relationship Over Six Weeks

Six weeks is long enough to build genuine trust and short enough that every session matters. The relationship dynamics that most affect coaching outcomes:

  • Trust is built in the first session. Your delivery of the pre-session assessment is the first trust signal. If it is honest, specific, and delivered with genuine belief in the candidate, the relationship starts on the right foundation.
  • Consistency is the highest deposit. Being prepared, being on time, reviewing their Loom submissions before the session, remembering what you covered last week — these behaviors make deposits in the coaching relationship that enable you to make withdrawals when you need to deliver hard feedback.
  • The candidate's crew words matter. When a candidate tells you that their crew would describe them as "service, humility, innovation" — those three words are more valuable data than any self-assessment score. Reference them. Use them. Connect the coaching work back to those words regularly.

Department Cohort Facilitation

How to structure, deliver, and close a department group coaching engagement — from first contact with the chief to final debrief.

The Department Cohort Value Proposition

Individual coaching produces individual promotions. Department cohort coaching produces better promotional processes, stronger candidate fields, and organizational improvement that extends beyond the candidates themselves. When you work with a department cohort you are simultaneously developing individual candidates and helping the department understand what they are actually selecting for when they promote.

The fire chief or training officer who hires you for a cohort engagement is investing in organizational quality — not just individual outcomes. Frame your proposal and your delivery in those terms.

The Cohort Engagement Structure

Phase 1 — Discovery (Before Contract)

Use the department inquiry form at tpscoachinglab.com to capture initial interest. Before quoting pricing, schedule a discovery call with the decision-maker — chief, training officer, or union rep. On that call, understand: How many candidates? What ranks? What is the assessment timeline? Have they tested before and what were the outcomes? What does the chief believe is the primary development gap in their field?

The chief's answer to that last question is gold. It tells you what the organization believes about its people and reveals where your coaching will be most valuable.

Phase 2 — Intake and Assessment (Week 1)

Send every candidate the intake form. Generate individual AI assessments for each candidate. Generate the cohort report (once built in the dashboard). Before the first group session, you will know:

  • The cohort-wide dimensional strengths and gaps
  • The bucket vulnerabilities shared across the field
  • Which candidates are most and least prepared
  • The common framework knowledge gaps across all candidates

Phase 3 — Group Sessions (Weeks 2–5)

Session 1
TPS System Orientation
Introduce the four dimensions, the nine buckets, and the 1–5 scoring scale. This is not assumed knowledge — even candidates who have tested before benefit from hearing the scoring framework explicitly. Run one group oral board scenario. Score it live and explain your scoring out loud. This single exercise produces more immediate development insight than any amount of lecture.
Session 2
Supervisory Judgment + Personnel Issues
The 3 U's Diagnostic in depth. Small group practice (3-4 candidates per group). Peer feedback with coaching facilitation. The group dynamic creates accountability that individual sessions cannot replicate — candidates hear the gaps in their peers' responses and recognize them in their own.
Session 3
Interpersonal Skills + Conflict
Glasl Escalation Ladder and IBR in depth. Role play with you playing the difficult party. Group debrief — what did candidates observe about each other's responses? The peer observation component produces insight that coach-only feedback cannot.
Session 4
Leadership Philosophy + Written Exercise
T·C·H·O·E·D·S and identity architecture. Written exercise intensive — all candidates write simultaneously, timed, then compare responses in pairs. The Five-Paragraph Formula applied to department-specific scenarios.

Phase 4 — Group Mock Assessment (Week 6)

The final group session is a full mock assessment center. Every candidate participates. You score every response. Provide written individual feedback within 48 hours of the session. This deliverable — individual written assessor feedback from someone who has sat on real assessment panels — is the highest-value output of the entire cohort engagement.

Phase 5 — Department Debrief (After Assessment)

After the actual promotional assessment is complete, offer to debrief with the chief or training officer. Share aggregate development outcomes. What the cohort improved. What gaps remained. Recommendations for the next cycle. This positions you as a long-term partner rather than a one-time vendor — and sets up the next cohort engagement.

Pricing and Scope Clarity

The $2,500 base price covers a cohort of up to 8 candidates. For larger cohorts use this structure: $2,500 for 1–8 candidates, $3,500 for 9–14 candidates, $5,000 for 15–20 candidates. Custom pricing above 20.

Always include in the contract: number of group sessions, individual intake assessments, one mock assessment session, written individual feedback, and the department debrief. Itemizing the deliverables makes the value concrete and prevents scope creep conversations.

The Cohort Coaching Difference

When you stand in front of a department cohort and score their practice responses live — explaining out loud what assessors are looking for and why — you are doing something that has never been done for those candidates. Most fire service promotional preparation is self-directed, isolated, and completely disconnected from the actual assessment process. You are bringing the assessor into the room. That experience is irreplaceable and it is yours alone to offer.
