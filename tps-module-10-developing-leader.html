The Four Levels of Competence:
Know Where They Are

⏱ 22 min
🎯 Supervisory Judgment · Self-Awareness · Development Planning

Before you can develop anyone, you have to know where they are. Not where you think they should be. Not where they were six months ago. Where they actually are right now — in their knowledge, in their skill, and in their awareness of the gap between the two.

The Four Levels of Competence — also called the Conscious Competence Model — gives you that map. Developed at Gordon Training International in the 1970s, it describes the four stages every learner passes through when developing any new skill. Once you can see which stage a crew member is in, you know exactly what kind of intervention they need. The wrong intervention for the right problem is not neutral — it actively delays development and damages the relationship between the supervisor and the person being developed.

The Four Levels — Applied to Fire/EMS Leadership

1
Level 1 — Unconscious Incompetence
You don't know what you don't know.
The firefighter who has never seen their leadership behavior scored by an assessor doesn't know their action step is vague — because they've never had a framework for what a specific action step looks like. The crew member who undermines morale doesn't perceive their own behavior as undermining because they've never received honest, specific behavioral feedback. The supervisor candidate who thinks they are ready because they "know the job" is here: competent at execution, unaware that assessment centers evaluate something entirely different. This is the most dangerous level — you cannot seek improvement for a gap you don't know exists.
Intervention: Revelation — show them the gap they cannot see
2
Level 2 — Conscious Incompetence
You know you don't know. This is the gateway.
The candidate who has watched their oral board recording for the first time and seen — specifically, unmistakably — that their action step describes what happened rather than naming the decision they made. The crew member who receives SBI feedback and, for the first time, understands how their behavior lands on the people around them. This level is uncomfortable. That discomfort is not a problem to be managed — it is the energy that drives development. Your job at Level 2 is to hold the honest picture steady and direct the discomfort toward practice. Don't rescue them from the recognition. That recognition is the beginning of everything.
Intervention: Practice — direct the energy toward deliberate repetition
3
Level 3 — Conscious Competence
You can do it when you're fully paying attention.
The candidate whose STAR structure holds when they are focused, prepared, and operating in a low-stress practice environment — but which fragments under assessment day pressure or an unexpected supplemental question. The crew member who gives excellent performance on a drill they knew was being observed, but whose habits revert when supervision is absent. Level 3 is genuinely competent but cognitively expensive: the skill still requires conscious construction, which means cognitive load under stress can collapse it. Level 3 under comfort is not the same as Level 3 under pressure. The goal is Level 4.
Intervention: Pressure — simulate the conditions that produce regression
4
Level 4 — Unconscious Competence
The skill executes automatically. This is the goal.
The action step is specific because specificity has become habitual — not constructed. The crew member who gives honest feedback to a colleague because that behavior is now who they are, not something they have to remember to do. In assessment center terms: the candidate who performs at 4/5 consistently across all exercises, including unseen questions and unexpected injects, because the behavioral habits are durable enough to survive assessment day stress. This is Phase 3 of the SPARR arc — Automaticity. The neuroscience basis: the skill has transferred from prefrontal cortex (deliberate) to basal ganglia (automatic) processing, which survives cognitive stress in a way that conscious construction does not.
Intervention: Challenge — push toward adjacent growth; prevent stagnation

The Counterintuitive Problem — When Experience Drives Incompetence to Level 4

Here is the insight that surprises almost every fire service candidate who encounters this model: it is entirely possible to develop unconscious incompetence — Level 1 — at Level 4 depth. Meaning: a behavior that is wrong has been practiced so many times, without feedback, that it has transferred to automatic execution. The behavior now runs without conscious awareness, which means it also runs without the candidate's ability to catch and correct it.

This is the explanation for the 20-year veteran who consistently underperforms in the assessment center against five-year candidates. They have not developed less — they have developed differently. They have 20 years of reinforcement for whatever habits they built, including the habits that do not score well. The vague action step has been executed thousands of times and is now deeply automatic. The implicit tactical communication that works perfectly on the fireground is now a deeply ingrained pattern that produces silence in assessment scenarios where verbal communication is the only scored behavior.

As a developing leader, your job is to identify which of your crew members are in this category — and to create the honest feedback environment that can move a deeply habituated Level 1 competence back to Level 2, where development becomes possible again. That feedback conversation is the hardest one in the development toolkit. The crew member with 20 years of experience is not going to welcome the news that they have been doing something wrong automatically for two decades.

"Experience without reflection produces expertise — just not always in the right things."

The SPARR Connection — This Is What You Are Already Doing

If you have been through the TPS SPARR framework, you have already been working through the Four Levels of Competence — you just had different vocabulary for it. Step 1 of SPARR (Spot the Moment) is the intervention for Level 1: making the gap visible so the candidate moves to Level 2. Step 3 (Actively Practice) is the Level 2 → Level 3 engine. Step 4 (Run It Live) is the Level 3 pressure test. And the entire arc from Phase 1 (Awareness) through Phase 2 (Application) to Phase 3 (Automaticity) is the journey from Level 2 through Level 3 to Level 4.

The vocabulary gives you two things: a language for conversations with your crew about where they are and what they need next, and a diagnostic tool for identifying which kind of coaching intervention a given person requires right now.

The Oral Board Application — Both Directions
This framework works in two directions in your assessment. First, as a candidate: understanding the Four Levels of Competence explains your own development arc and gives you language to describe your preparation methodology with sophistication that most candidates cannot match. Second, as a future supervisor demonstrating development competency: when asked "how do you assess and develop your crew members," the candidate who describes diagnosing which level a crew member is at before deciding on an intervention scores at a fundamentally different level than the candidate who says "I identify training needs and address them." One is a framework. The other is a platitude.
Oral Board — Development Competency Questions
Layer 1
"How do you assess the development needs of a new crew member when you take over a company?"
A Four Levels answer: "I start by determining whether gaps are at the awareness level — does this person know what good performance looks like? — or at the execution level — do they know but can't yet do it consistently? Those require different interventions."
Layer 2
"Tell me about a time you had to give feedback to an experienced crew member who didn't know they had a performance problem. How did you approach it and what happened?"
This question specifically tests Level 1 intervention competency — moving someone from Unconscious Incompetence to Conscious Incompetence. The 4/5 answer describes specific behavioral observation, a private conversation, and the specific language used to make the gap visible without being punitive.
Layer 3
"A highly experienced crew member consistently performs below expectations in training scenarios but performs well on actual calls. How would you approach their development?"
Lesson 1 — Key Takeaways
The Four Levels of Competence maps any skill development journey: Unconscious Incompetence → Conscious Incompetence → Conscious Competence → Unconscious Competence. The level determines the intervention — not the supervisor's preference or the trainee's experience level.
Level 1 (Unconscious Incompetence) at deep experience levels is the most challenging development scenario. 20 years of unreflected practice can encode wrong habits at automaticity depth. Moving someone from L1 back to L2 requires honest, specific behavioral feedback — the hardest conversation in the development toolkit.
Level 2 discomfort is the energy that drives development. Do not rescue crew members from the recognition of their gap. Hold the honest picture steady and direct the energy toward practice.
Level 3 under comfort is not the same as Level 3 under pressure. The goal is always Level 4 — habits durable enough to survive stress without conscious reconstruction. That is what SPARR's pressure simulation phase is building.
This framework is oral board material in both directions: to describe your own development arc, and to demonstrate supervisory development sophistication when asked how you assess and develop your crew.

The Skill/Will Matrix:
The Right Diagnosis Before the Right Intervention

⏱ 22 min
👥 Supervisory Judgment · Interpersonal Skills · Personnel Scenarios

The most expensive mistake a company officer makes is applying the wrong intervention to the right problem. Sending someone to training when the issue is motivation. Coaching someone harder when the issue is that they genuinely cannot do the task. Counseling someone for attitude when the underlying issue is that they are bored and under-challenged. Every one of these errors wastes supervisory time, delays real improvement, and signals to the crew member — and to everyone watching — that their supervisor does not actually understand what is happening.

The Skill/Will Matrix, developed from Paul Hersey and Ken Blanchard's Situational Leadership research and refined by Max Landsberg in The Tao of Coaching, prevents that error. It maps every crew member's development situation on two axes — their current skill level for a specific task, and their current motivation to perform that task — and produces four quadrants, each with a different and specific supervisory response.

The Matrix — Four Quadrants, Four Different Conversations

↑ HIGH SKILL  |  LOW SKILL ↓     ← LOW WILL  |  HIGH WILL →
High Skill · High Will
The Star
Delegate + Stretch
This person can do the job well and wants to. The supervisor's role: get out of their way, give them autonomy, stretch them toward the next level. The most common mismanagement here is hovering — micro-managing a capable, motivated person destroys engagement faster than almost any other behavior. The greatest risk with Stars is neglect: because they don't generate problems, they receive less supervisory investment, and eventually less investment becomes the signal that there's no future here.
→ Delegate, stretch, develop toward next role. Check in; don't check up.
Low Skill · High Will
The Enthusiast
Direct + Coach
High energy, undertrained. This person wants to perform well and doesn't yet have the capability. The intervention is training, coaching, and close direction — with the explicit recognition that enthusiasm is the asset to protect. Do not mistake enthusiasm for competence — the Enthusiast who is given autonomy before developing competence will fail publicly and lose the enthusiasm you needed. Do not mistake incompetence for unwillingness — the Enthusiast who receives discipline instead of development loses the one thing they had going for them.
→ Direct clearly, train specifically, praise effort, coach gaps. Never discipline what should be trained.
High Skill · Low Will
The Cynic
Diagnose First
This person can do the job and isn't. Before any intervention, diagnose the root cause — because the wrong intervention here is more damaging than anywhere else. Is this boredom from under-challenge? A blocked promotional path? Organizational disillusionment? A personal situation affecting performance? Each cause has a different response. Sending a capable-but-disengaged person to more training is an insult. Having a direct conversation about what is actually happening may be the only thing that re-engages them — or it may surface that the situation is irresolvable, which is also useful information.
→ Diagnose the will gap before prescribing. Ask, don't assume. Then respond to the actual cause.
Low Skill · Low Will
The Problem?
Assess Carefully
Before writing this person off: is this a won't or a can't? A new crew member who is overwhelmed in the first month may present as low will when the actual issue is that the gap between their current capability and their expectations of themselves is producing shutdown, not disengagement. A fundamentally mis-hired person is a different situation entirely. Distinguish carefully. The new, overwhelmed, self-critical crew member needs support and early wins. The genuinely unwilling-and-unable person needs a different kind of conversation.
→ Separate can't from won't. Support the overwhelmed; accountability conversation for the disengaged.

The Most Common Misdiagnosis Errors — Know These Before Your Assessment

Assessment center role play exercises and personnel scenario questions are specifically designed to test whether you make these errors. Assessors know the patterns. If your response to a personnel scenario demonstrates one of these misdiagnoses, it will score at 2/5 regardless of how good your communication style is.

Error 1: Treating a Skill problem as a Will problem. The crew member who is performing below standard because they lack training receives a counseling conversation about attitude. Result: they feel blamed for something that was not their fault, they become less willing, and the skill gap remains unaddressed. The diagnostic question: have they ever been shown specifically what good performance looks like? If no, this is a Skill problem until proven otherwise.

Error 2: Treating a Will problem as a Skill problem. The capable, disengaged crew member receives more training. Result: they feel patronized — "you're sending me to training, as if I don't know how to do this?" — and become more disengaged. The diagnostic question: can they demonstrate the skill when they choose to? If yes, skill is not the issue.

Error 3: Neglecting the High Skill/High Will person. Stars don't generate supervisory problems, so they receive less investment. Over time, Stars stop trying, stop growing, and eventually leave or check out. The diagnostic question: when did I last have a development conversation — not a performance conversation, a development conversation — with my best performer?

The Critical Insight — Task Specificity
The Skill/Will Matrix is task-specific, not person-specific. The same crew member can be High Skill/High Will on fire operations and High Skill/Low Will on EMS documentation — because motivation is situational, not fixed. A person who is disengaged on one type of work may be highly engaged on another. The supervisor who diagnoses someone as universally Low Will based on one observation set has made a category error. Always apply the matrix to a specific task or performance area, not to the person as a whole.

Connecting Skill/Will to the Four Levels

The Skill/Will Matrix and the Four Levels of Competence are complementary diagnostics. The Four Levels tells you where someone is in their development journey for a skill. The Skill/Will Matrix tells you what kind of supervisory response that position requires right now. Together, they produce a complete picture:

  • Level 1 (Unconscious Incompetence) often presents as High Will / Low Skill in the Skill/Will Matrix — the enthusiastic person who doesn't know what they're missing. Revelation + coaching.
  • Level 2 (Conscious Incompetence) is the hardest Skill/Will moment — the person who now knows they have a gap. Their will may temporarily drop as the recognition lands. Patience, specific practice assignments, early wins.
  • Level 3 (Conscious Competence) is High Skill / variable Will depending on conditions. Pressure simulation moves them toward Level 4. Monitor for the will dip that comes from repeated pressure without visible progress.
  • Level 4 (Unconscious Competence) ideally presents as High Skill / High Will — the Star. But watch for Level 4 becoming a comfort zone. The developing leader's job is to introduce adjacent challenges that start a new Level 1 → Level 4 cycle in a neighboring skill area.

The 3 U's:
Unable, Unaware, Unwilling

⏱ 20 min
🔍 Root Cause Diagnosis · Role Play · Personnel Scenarios

Every performance problem in the fire service traces back to one of three root causes. Not two. Not five. Three. And the intervention that produces improvement is different for each one — which means that getting the diagnosis wrong produces the wrong intervention, which produces no improvement, which produces more frustration, which often produces discipline where development should have happened.

The 3 U's is the simplest and most immediately applicable root cause diagnostic framework in the performance management literature. Before you have any performance conversation, before you choose your intervention, before you decide whether this is a training matter or a discipline matter — you ask which U you are dealing with.

U
Unable
They want to, but can't. This is a skill or knowledge gap. The crew member who wants to perform well and doesn't have the capability — the technical skill, the procedural knowledge, the behavioral habit — that the performance requires. Unable is not a character flaw. It is a development stage. The correct response is always training, coaching, and supported practice — never discipline, never counseling for attitude, never performance improvement plans that punish the gap without addressing its cause.
In assessment terms: the candidate who wants to perform well in their oral board but whose STAR structure collapses under pressure is Unable, not Unwilling. The intervention is SPARR, not motivation. Knowing this distinction is the difference between a coach who produces improvement and one who produces frustration.
Intervention: Train · Coach · Practice · Support · Early Wins
U
Unaware
They don't know there's a problem. This is a self-awareness and feedback gap. The crew member whose behavior is producing negative consequences they have never been told about. The candidate who doesn't know their action step is vague because they have never had it named specifically. The supervisor whose communication style is damaging team trust while they believe the team is performing well. Unaware cannot fix what they cannot see. The correct response is honest, specific, behaviorally grounded feedback — delivered in a way that makes the gap visible without triggering defensiveness so severe that the information cannot be received.
Unaware is the category that most supervisors avoid addressing. The conversation that makes someone Consciously Incompetent is uncomfortable for both parties. But leaving someone in Unconscious Incompetence — in Unaware — is not kindness. It is the slow-motion guarantee that they will eventually face consequences for a problem they never had the information to correct.
Intervention: Honest SBI Feedback · Make the Gap Visible · Hold the Picture Steady
U
Unwilling
They know, they can, and they won't. This is a motivation or values gap — and it is the hardest category because neither training nor feedback resolves it. The crew member who is fully aware of the standard, demonstrably capable of meeting it, and choosing not to. This is the only U where accountability structures, consequences, and formal discipline are the appropriate intervention. But — and this is critical — you must be certain you are dealing with Unwilling before you bring those tools to bear. Treating an Unable person as Unwilling is the most damaging supervisory error in the fire service. It communicates that the supervisor sees a bad person where there is actually a developing one.
The test for Unwilling: can they do this when they choose to? If the answer is clearly yes, and they are consistently choosing not to, you are dealing with Unwilling. The intervention is a direct, accountability-framed conversation that names the standard, the behavior, the gap between them, and the consequences of continued non-performance.
Intervention: Direct Accountability Conversation · Clear Consequences · Documentation

The Diagnostic Sequence — Three Questions Before Every Performance Conversation

Before any performance conversation, run this sequence in your own analysis. Not out loud, not as a script for the conversation — as your internal diagnostic before you decide what kind of conversation to have.

  1. Have they ever been shown specifically what good performance looks like? If no, check Unaware first. If they have never received specific behavioral feedback about this gap, they may be operating with a completely different understanding of what is expected. The Unaware diagnosis requires addressing first.
  2. Do they have the skill and knowledge to perform at the required level? If no, check Unable. Even if they have been told about the standard, if they don't have the developmental foundation to meet it, training and coaching are the intervention — not consequences.
  3. Given that they know the standard and can meet it — are they meeting it? If no consistently and clearly, and both of the above have been addressed, you are dealing with Unwilling. Now accountability structures are appropriate.
The Costliest Diagnostic Error in Fire Service Supervision
Defaulting to Unwilling. The fire service culture — built around accountability, standards, and personal responsibility — has a structural bias toward interpreting performance gaps as motivation failures. When a crew member underperforms, the default cultural explanation is that they don't care, they're not trying, they're being difficult. In reality, most first-time performance gaps in the fire service are Unable or Unaware — not Unwilling. The supervisor who defaults to the Unwilling interpretation sends the Unable crew member to discipline, which produces resentment, legal exposure, and a performance gap that continues to compound because its actual cause was never addressed.

The 3 U's and the Assessment Center — Direct Application

The 3 U's shows up directly in two assessment center exercise types. In role play exercises involving a crew member performance issue, the assessors are watching whether you gather enough information to determine which U you are dealing with before deciding on your response. The candidate who walks in and immediately addresses attitude (Unwilling assumption) without first exploring whether the crew member knows what good performance looks like (Unaware check) or whether they have the skill to deliver it (Unable check) is demonstrating exactly the diagnostic failure the exercise is designed to surface.

In in-basket exercises involving personnel situations, the quality of your written response is evaluated against whether your proposed action is appropriate for the type of problem described. A documentation-and-discipline response to what is clearly an Unable situation scores at 2/5. A training-and-coaching response to what is clearly an Unwilling situation scores at 2/5. Getting the diagnosis right first is the prerequisite for the right response.

The Four Levels of Competence, the Skill/Will Matrix, and the 3 U's are three views of the same development landscape. Use them together:

Four Levels tells you where they are in the skill acquisition journey. Skill/Will tells you what combination of capability and motivation is present right now for this specific task. 3 U's tells you why the gap exists, which determines what kind of conversation to have.

The 70-20-10 Model & Adult Learning:
How Development Actually Happens

⏱ 22 min
📈 Crew Development · Transformational Leadership · Development Planning

When an assessor asks you "how do you develop your crew?" — what percentage of the development landscape does your answer cover?

Most fire service candidates describe formal training programs, certifications, and structured courses. That answer is accurate, complete, and describes approximately 10% of how adults actually develop. The 90% they are missing — the on-the-job experiences, the mentoring relationships, the real-time feedback loops — is where the majority of genuine professional development happens. And it is the 90% that a company officer has the most direct influence over.

The 70-20-10 Model — Development in Its True Proportions

70%
On-the-Job Experience — The Development Engine
Structured, intentional, reflected experience at work
Stretch assignments. New responsibilities that push the edge of current capability. Projects that require working across silos. Real decisions with real consequences and real feedback. The 70% is not simply "time on the job" — unstructured, unreflected experience does not produce development. The company officer's role in the 70% is to make it intentional: assign the stretch task deliberately, debrief it specifically, and connect the experience to the developmental goal explicitly. The crew member who handles their first difficult personnel conversation with coaching and a structured debrief afterward develops faster than the one who handles it alone and never processes what happened.
20%
Learning from Others — The Relationship Engine
Mentoring, coaching, observing, and feedback from peers and supervisors
The developmental conversations that happen in the kitchen after a significant call. The senior officer who takes a crew member through their thinking on a difficult decision. The peer who gives honest, specific feedback that no formal training program would ever provide. The 20% is relationship-mediated learning — and the company officer who builds a crew culture of genuine feedback and mentoring is investing in the 20% that produces some of the highest-leverage development available. Your Field Record, your post-incident conversations, your monthly one-on-ones with crew members — this is 20% work.
10%
Formal Training — The Foundation Layer
Courses, certifications, classes, and structured learning programs
Necessary but insufficient. Formal training provides the vocabulary, the frameworks, and the baseline knowledge that the 70% and 20% then develop into genuine capability. The crew member who attends an officer development course and returns to a shift where nothing changes — no application, no mentoring, no stretch assignments — retains perhaps 10% of what they learned within two weeks. The crew member who returns to a supervisor who asks "what was the most important thing you took from that course, and how are we going to apply it here?" develops something durable.
The Oral Board Implication — Cover the Full 100%
When asked how you develop your crew, the candidate who describes only formal training (the 10%) is demonstrating awareness of a fraction of the development landscape. The candidate who describes structured on-the-job development assignments (70%), mentoring relationships and feedback culture (20%), and targeted formal training with explicit application bridges (10%) is demonstrating full-spectrum development thinking. That answer scores at 5/5. It also happens to be what actually produces results — which is the best reason to build these practices before the assessment, not just to describe them in it.

Malcolm Knowles and Andragogy — Why Adult Learning Is Different

Malcolm Knowles coined the term andragogy — the art and science of helping adults learn — to distinguish it from pedagogy, the educational framework designed for children. His six principles of adult learning describe why fire service training so often produces compliance rather than competence, and what to do about it.

🎓
Knowles' Six Principles of Adult Learning — Fire Service Applied
Knowles, 1970

1. Adults are self-directed. Adults need autonomy in their learning process. Fire service training that prescribes every element without allowing the learner any input into direction, pacing, or method produces resentment and compliance. The most effective development conversations start with the crew member's own assessment of where they want to go — not the supervisor's prescription of where they need to be.

2. Adults bring experience as a resource. The 20-year firefighter in your development conversation brings two decades of relevant experience. Training that ignores this — that treats experienced adults like blank slates — wastes the most valuable resource in the room and signals disrespect. The best fire service training starts with drawing on what participants already know, then building from there.

3. Adults learn based on readiness — they need to be ready to learn. A crew member who is not yet in a developmental stage where a particular learning is relevant will not retain it regardless of the quality of the instruction. This is why the Four Levels of Competence matters: a Level 1 crew member is not ready for advanced coaching on a gap they do not yet know they have.

4. Adults are problem-centered, not subject-centered. Adults learn best when training is organized around real problems they face, not around academic subjects. "How do you handle a crew member who is consistently late?" produces more learning than "here are the principles of progressive discipline." Fire service training that connects directly to real operational or supervisory challenges the learner is currently navigating is dramatically more effective than training organized around compliance categories.

5. Adults are internally motivated. External motivation — mandatory training hours, certification requirements — produces attendance. Internal motivation — genuine curiosity, clear connection to professional goals, personal investment in the outcome — produces learning. The company officer who helps crew members connect their development to what they actually care about professionally is investing in the internal motivation that produces genuine growth.

6. Adults need to know WHY before HOW. The crew member who does not understand why a protocol exists will follow it under supervision and deviate when not supervised. The one who understands the why owns the standard even when no one is watching. Every training conversation should answer the why question before it addresses the how.

The Leadership Connection Knowles' principle #6 — adults need WHY before HOW — is the pedagogical description of Transformational Leadership's Intellectual Stimulation dimension. The company officer who explains the reasoning behind standards, who invites questions about the why, and who treats the crew as adults capable of understanding the rationale for what they are asked to do is practicing andragogy and transformational leadership simultaneously. These are not separate frameworks. They are the same leadership behavior described from two different research traditions.

Bloom's Taxonomy:
Training That Actually Transfers

⏱ 20 min
🧩 Training Design · Intellectual Stimulation · Assessment Readiness

Benjamin Bloom and a committee of educational psychologists published their taxonomy of educational objectives in 1956. Revised in 2001 by a former student, it remains the most widely used framework in instructional design worldwide. For a company officer designing crew training, it answers a question that most fire service training ignores: at what cognitive level are we actually training?

The answer matters because assessment center oral board questions operate at the upper levels of Bloom's taxonomy — Analyze, Evaluate, and Create. Most fire service training delivers content at the lower levels — Remember and Understand. When the gap between how people are trained and how they are tested is that large, preparation fails not because of effort but because of level mismatch.

The Six Levels — From Recall to Creation

1
Remember
Recall facts, definitions, and basic information from memory.
Fire service example: "What are the four dimensions assessed in a company officer assessment center?" Most training lives here. Most retention is lost within 72 hours without reinforcement.
2
Understand
Interpret, classify, and explain ideas or concepts in your own words.
Fire service example: "Explain in your own words why the action step is the most important element of a STAR response." Understanding is not application. Most training stops here and calls it competence.
3
Apply
Use information or a concept in a new situation.
Fire service example: "Record yourself answering this unseen behavioral question and identify where your action step is specific versus vague." This is where drill-based training lives when it is working. The 70% on-the-job experience tier operates here.
4
Analyze
Break information into parts, identify patterns, causes, and relationships.
Fire service example: "Watch this video of an oral board response. Identify the specific moments where the structure breaks down and explain what is causing each failure." This is where most oral board questions begin. The candidate who can only Remember and Understand cannot Answer at this level under pressure.
5
Evaluate
Make judgments about the value or quality of information, options, or methods.
Fire service example: "Given this personnel scenario, evaluate the three possible responses and justify which one best serves the crew, the individual, and the organization simultaneously." Supplemental questions operate here. The candidate who cannot evaluate under time pressure is demonstrating the level mismatch.
6
Create
Combine information to produce something new — a plan, a product, a solution.
Fire service example: "Design a 90-day crew development plan for a newly promoted company officer taking over a crew with significant performance and morale challenges." This is the level that distinguishes the top-ranked candidate from the competitive field. Assessment centers reward Create-level responses.

The Training Level Mismatch — Why Competent People Fail Assessments

The level mismatch is the most common explanation for the counterintuitive assessment result: the genuinely knowledgeable, experienced candidate who performs poorly in the assessment center. They have trained at Levels 1 and 2 — they know the content, they can explain it. The assessment is operating at Levels 4 and 5. The gap is not a knowledge gap. It is a cognitive level gap.

The TPS SPARR framework addresses this directly: the deliberate practice sessions that produce Level 3 competence (Apply), the peer review and self-evaluation that produce Level 4 competence (Analyze), the coaching debrief that produces Level 5 competence (Evaluate), and the oral resume and development planning questions that require Level 6 competence (Create). If you are only practicing at the Understand level — studying frameworks without applying, analyzing, or evaluating them — you are preparing for a different assessment than the one you will take.

Designing Training at the Right Level
As a company officer designing crew training, Bloom's gives you a quality check: at what level is this training actually operating? A training session that asks crew members to state the four steps of a search pattern (Level 1) is categorically different from one that asks them to analyze a simulated search scenario and evaluate the decisions made against the protocol (Level 4-5). The former produces recall. The latter produces judgment. Operational competence requires judgment. Design your training at the level you actually need, not at the level that is easiest to teach.
Oral Board — Training Design & Bloom's Application
Layer 1
"How do you design training for your crew that produces genuine operational improvement rather than just compliance with training requirements?"
Bloom's answer: "I design training at the Apply and Analyze levels — not just Remember and Understand. Crew members need to practice decisions in conditions that resemble the real ones, not just recall information in a classroom."
Layer 2
"Tell me about a training session or program you designed or delivered that produced measurable improvement in your crew's performance. What made it effective?"
Lesson 5 — Key Takeaways
Bloom's six levels: Remember → Understand → Apply → Analyze → Evaluate → Create. Most fire service training operates at 1 and 2. Most assessment center questions operate at 4 and 5. That gap — not lack of effort — is why competent people fail assessments.
Designing training at the Apply and Analyze levels produces more operational competence than any amount of additional content at the Remember and Understand levels. The question is not "what do they know" — it is "at what level can they use what they know under pressure?"
SPARR addresses the level mismatch deliberately: practice sessions build Apply competence, self-evaluation builds Analyze competence, coaching debriefs build Evaluate competence, development planning builds Create competence. Know which level each practice activity is targeting.
The Six Levels
1. Remember
Recall facts. Where most training lives. 70% forgotten within 72 hours without reinforcement.
2. Understand
Explain in own words. Not application. Where most training stops and calls it competence.
3. Apply
Use in a new situation. Where effective drill training operates.
4. Analyze
Break apart, identify patterns. Where most oral board questions begin.
5. Evaluate
Make judgments about quality or value. Where supplemental questions operate.
6. Create
Combine to produce something new. Where top-ranked candidates operate.
The Level Mismatch
Training at 1-2 + assessment at 4-5 = predictable failure regardless of knowledge level. Close the gap by practicing at 3-5.

SBI Feedback & Kolb's Cycle:
Closing the Learning Loop

⏱ 22 min
💬 Feedback · Documentation · Learning Culture · Interpersonal Skills

Development without feedback is experience. It is valuable — but it is not learning. The difference between a crew member who grows from a challenging call and one who simply survives it is the quality of the feedback conversation that follows. This lesson covers the two frameworks that convert experience into durable learning: the SBI feedback model, which structures feedback so it can actually be received, and Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle, which explains why experience alone is never enough.

The SBI Feedback Model — Situation, Behavior, Impact

Developed by the Center for Creative Leadership, the SBI model answers a simple question: why does so much well-intentioned feedback produce defensiveness rather than growth? The answer is specificity. Most fire service feedback is impressionistic — it describes the supervisor's general evaluation of the person rather than the specific observable behavior that caused a specific observable impact. Impressionistic feedback gives the recipient nothing to act on and nothing to defend against except the general evaluation, which they will naturally resist.

S — Situation
Set the specific context. When and where did this happen? Be precise enough that both parties are referring to the same event. "Last Tuesday's shift briefing, when we were reviewing the new EMS protocol" — not "sometimes in meetings." Specificity at the Situation level prevents the recipient from dismissing the feedback as a general pattern they can argue doesn't reflect the full picture.
B — Behavior
Describe the observable behavior — not the interpretation. What did they say or do that you could have recorded on video? "You interrupted FF Martinez twice while she was speaking, and each time redirected the conversation before she finished her point" — not "you seemed dismissive" or "you have a communication problem." Observable behavior is inarguable. Interpretations invite debate. The behavior description is the part of SBI that most supervisors rush past — because it requires them to have actually observed specifically rather than formed a general impression.
I — Impact
Describe the specific consequence of that behavior. Not what you felt about it — what it produced. "Martinez did not contribute again for the rest of the briefing, and two other crew members pulled back after her second interruption" — not "it created a bad vibe." The Impact statement is the bridge between behavior and consequence that helps the feedback recipient understand why the behavior matters. Without it, the feedback feels like personal preference. With it, it becomes operational data about what the behavior is actually producing.

SBI Applied — Two Examples

Impressionistic version (2/5): "You need to work on your communication style with crew members. Sometimes you come across as dismissive and it affects team morale."

SBI version (4/5): "In Tuesday's briefing when we were reviewing the new EMS protocol, you interrupted FF Martinez twice while she was mid-sentence, each time redirecting the conversation before she finished her point. After the second interruption, she didn't contribute again for the rest of the briefing, and I noticed two other crew members also pulled back. I want to talk about what I observed there."

The SBI version gives the recipient something specific to respond to, something specific to change, and something that is legally defensible if it becomes the basis for documentation. The impressionistic version gives them nothing except the option to disagree with the general evaluation.

SBI and STAR — The Same Structure
Notice the structural parallel: SBI feedback and STAR oral board responses share the same organizing principle — specificity about context, observable behavior/action, and consequence/impact. The candidate who has learned to construct STAR responses has already learned the skeleton of SBI feedback. The company officer who practices giving SBI feedback is building the same specificity habits that produce 4/5 STAR responses. These are not separate skills. They are the same discipline applied in two directions: STAR describes what you did; SBI describes what you observed in someone else.

Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle — Why Experience Alone Is Never Enough

David Kolb's Experiential Learning Theory describes learning as a four-stage cycle. The critical insight: the cycle requires all four stages to produce genuine learning. Experience alone — without reflection, without conceptualization, without active application — produces familiarity, not development. This is the explanation for the fire service phenomenon of the highly experienced person who makes the same mistakes year after year: they are cycling through Concrete Experience repeatedly without completing the full learning cycle.

1
Concrete Experience
The event itself. The call, the difficult conversation, the training scenario, the assessment exercise. This is where fire service development typically begins and, for many practitioners, where it ends. Experience without the next three stages produces pattern reinforcement, not development.
2
Reflective Observation
What happened? What did I observe — in the situation, in my response, in the result? This is the stage that most fire service development skips. The after action review is the institutional mechanism for this stage. Without reflection, the concrete experience simply reinforces whatever pattern already existed.
3
Abstract Conceptualization
What principle or framework explains what happened? What general insight can I draw from this specific experience? This is where the frameworks in this module become tools rather than theories — they are the vocabulary for converting a specific experience into a transferable principle.
4
Active Experimentation
What will I do differently in the next similar situation? The concrete commitment to apply the learning. Without this stage, reflection becomes rumination. The behavioral change — the next STAR practice rep, the next feedback conversation, the next stretch assignment — is the evidence that learning has actually occurred.

Kolb's Cycle in the Fire Service Development System
Kolb, 1984

The After Action Review from Module 9 is Kolb's cycle institutionalized: the AAR's four questions (What was the plan? What happened? Why the difference? What do we do next time?) map exactly to Kolb's four stages. A crew that AARs consistently is completing the full learning cycle after every significant event. A crew that doesn't is looping through Stage 1 — Concrete Experience — over and over, building familiarity without building development.

The Field Record is also Kolb's cycle in miniature: recording the Situation (Concrete Experience), capturing your Reflection on the decision (Reflective Observation), connecting it to a principle (Abstract Conceptualization), and identifying the next application (Active Experimentation). Every Field Record entry that contains all four elements is a complete learning cycle documented.

The Integrated Development System When you can describe your development system using the frameworks across this module — 70-20-10 structures the investment, Bloom's ensures the right cognitive level, SBI provides the feedback mechanism, and Kolb's cycle ensures each experience produces learning rather than just familiarity — you are describing a development system that assessors can evaluate as sophisticated, research-grounded, and operationally complete. That answer scores at 5/5 for every development question in the assessment.
