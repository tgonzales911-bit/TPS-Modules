The WE Mindset &
The First National Bank of Leadership

⏱ 22 min
🧠 Mindset + Framework
📋 Field Record exercise included

Before you learn a single tactic for your oral board, before you touch a STAR response or an in-basket item, you need to solve a language problem. It is the first thing I address with every new leader I coach — and it is the one change that most immediately signals to everyone around you, including an assessment panel, that something real has shifted in how you see your role.

The problem is the word "I."

There Is No I — There Is Only WE

When you step into a leadership role, you are no longer the individual contributor. You are the representative of the team. The way your language reflects that shift — or fails to — is observable in every interaction you have. Assessors hear it. Your crew hears it. The people above you hear it.

Here is the rule we carry in our department culture, and one we want you to carry for the rest of this course:

"The only time you should use 'I' is when you are taking accountability or responsibility for a failure of the team. Every other sentence should be WE."

— Leading with Purpose · The WE Principle

Think about what that means in practice. "I handled that MCI well" becomes "our crew handled that MCI well." "I solved the scheduling problem" becomes "we worked out the scheduling problem." "I developed a new training protocol" becomes "we built a new training protocol together." Notice what changes: credit becomes shared. Ownership becomes collective. The team gets built up every time you speak.

Now notice when the "I" comes back: "I take full responsibility for what happened with that call. That is on me." Here, the singular first person is exactly right — because you are carrying the weight of leadership, not distributing it. That is the only appropriate use of the word "I" once you take the rank.

This is not just cultural nicety. The research on team cohesion, psychological ownership, and organizational trust consistently shows that leaders whose language reflects collective ownership build stronger, more loyal, more engaged teams than those whose language reflects individual achievement. The fire service version of this is a phrase that runs through our department culture: there are no bad teams, only bad leaders. If your team is not performing, the language you examine first is your own.

The Mindfulness Practice
I am not asking you to attend a retreat or sit in a sweat lodge. I am asking you to set one intention every morning for the next thirty days: notice every time you use "I" in a leadership context and ask yourself whether it should be "we." Do this until it becomes automatic. Mindfulness in leadership starts with something this simple and this powerful. When "WE" becomes your default, you will have changed something real about how you lead.

The First National Bank of Leadership

Now that your language is right, let's talk about what leadership actually is — not the title, not the badge, not the rank. Leadership, at its most fundamental level, is the building of equity. We call it the First National Bank of Leadership, and we want you to think about your career inside this metaphor from this point forward.

Every action you take as a leader either deposits into your leadership account or withdraws from it. The people who follow you — your crew, your peers, your supervisors, your department — are watching both sides of the ledger. When you have made enough deposits over time, you have built what we call followership. Followership is the equity that lets you do the hard things when hard things are required. It is what gets people moving when you give direction without having to explain yourself at length. It is what makes a crew run into a burning building because they trust the person sending them in.

And when you run out of it — when you have withdrawn more than you have deposited — you become what every one of us has worked for at some point in our career: a bankrupt leader. You know who I am talking about. You have worked for them. Everything is a fight. Directives get questioned and undercut. The crew is always a step behind. Nobody is bringing their best to the shift because the account is empty.

DEPOSITS — Building Equity
Competence — being genuinely good at the job
Reliability — doing what you said you would do
Showing up for your people off-duty
Doing the emergency standbys without complaint
Knocking out crew chores while the medic unit is getting slammed
Making the right call on a tough EMS situation
Being the peacemaker during crew conflict
Sticking up for people who are not in the room
Being the glue — what we call the crewnity anchor
Positivity that is genuine, not performed
Resilience when things go wrong
WITHDRAWALS — Spending Equity
Incompetence — especially if you pretend otherwise
Unreliability — not following through
Negativity — chronic, corrosive, contagious
Selfishness — the ME focus
Arrogance — rank used as identity
Insincerity — fake leadership, promotivation
Inflexibility — my way regardless of evidence
Insecurity — punishing people for being capable
Taking credit for the team's work

Always Be Putting Money in the Bank

The rule is simple: ALWAYS BE MAKING DEPOSITS. Not because you are keeping score. Not because you are calculating what you will get back. Because the account you build over the next five to ten years of your career is the account you will draw on when you need your people to follow you into the hard situations — the policy nobody likes, the personnel decision that costs you a friendship, the call that goes sideways and the crew needs you steady. You cannot make those withdrawals if the account is empty.

Notice what is on the deposits list: going to the baby shower. Helping a teammate move. Doing the standbys. Knocking out the chores. None of this is heroic. None of it shows up in your promotional packet. It is the invisible ledger that your crew maintains about you — and every single person on that crew knows exactly what your balance is, even if they have never spoken it out loud.

The Oral Board Application
When an assessor asks you about a time you influenced someone who did not report to you, or built a relationship across a difficult political environment, or got something done without formal authority — they are asking you to demonstrate your leadership account balance. The candidate who says "I convinced them by explaining the rationale" is demonstrating a 3-level response. The candidate who says "I had spent two years building a relationship with that crew, and when I came to them with this, they trusted where it was coming from" is demonstrating 4/5 level followership equity in action. Your Field Record is where you start building the story library for this.

Crewnity — The Word That Came Out of Our Culture

The term crewnity grew out of our department culture to describe the combination of crew + unity that produces a shift that actually functions as a team. It is the glue — the intangible quality of a group of people who have decided they are in this together. You know what crewnity feels like from the inside. You know what the absence of it feels like too.

As a company officer, crewnity is your primary deliverable. Your tactical competence is table stakes — you are supposed to know your job. What makes a company officer exceptional is the crewnity of the crew they build and maintain. That is what transfers. That is what protects your people. And that is what assessment centers are trying to identify when they ask you about team dynamics, conflict resolution, and crew leadership.

Fandom, Followership,
and the Framing Effect

⏱ 24 min
🧠 Framework + Culture Analysis
📊 Followership Matrix included

Start with a question. Think about your favorite sports team, your favorite band, a movie or a show that you are genuinely invested in. Think about the last time you talked about it with someone who had no idea what you were referencing. Think about the energy you had explaining it. The loyalty. The willingness to defend it. The community you feel with other people who share it.

Now ask yourself honestly: when was the last time you brought that same energy to your job?

And if the honest answer is "a long time ago," stay with that for a moment before we go further — because that gap is exactly what this lesson is about.

The Followership Matrix — Where Are You?

Robert Kelley's followership research mapped five types of followers on two axes: how independently and critically they think, and how actively engaged they are. The five types that result — Sheep, Yes-People, Alienated Followers, Survivors, and Exemplary Followers — describe the distribution you will find in every fire department, in every shift, on every crew you ever lead.

Most fire service culture has an uncomfortable relationship with the upper-right quadrant — the Exemplary Follower. Kelley calls them Stars. In our department culture, we call them Fans and Believers, because the traditional terminology has a cultural problem we will address in a moment. They bring their best. They raise concerns through legitimate channels. They fully execute decisions they disagree with once those decisions are made. They make the organization better by their presence and they hold it accountable in ways that matter.

That is the quadrant you want to be in. And — more importantly for your role as a leader — that is the quadrant you want to build in your crew.

⬡
Kelley's Followership Matrix — Applied to Fire/EMS
Kelley, 1992
The matrix plots two axes: Independent Critical Thinking (vertical: high to low) and Active Engagement (horizontal: passive to active). The five resulting types and their fire service manifestation: Sheep (passive, dependent — shows up, does the minimum, no engagement); Yes-People (active engagement but dependent thinking — enthusiastic compliance, dangerous because they amplify bad decisions without questioning); Alienated Followers (independent thinkers but passive — the cynic in the corner who is often right but contributes nothing); Survivors (middle of both axes — the politically savvy operator who tracks the wind and adjusts accordingly); Exemplary Followers / Fans and Believers (high on both — independent, critical, actively engaged, fully committed).
The Oral Board Implication When assessors ask about followership, they are specifically trying to differentiate Exemplary Followers from Yes-People. "I always support my supervisor" is a Yes-Person answer. The Exemplary Follower answer includes a specific moment of genuine internal conflict — a concern raised privately, heard or not heard, and then fully executed either way. That narrative structure is what scores at 4/5. Find that story from your actual experience before your assessment.

What If Followership Was More Like Fandom?

Here is where I depart from the traditional followership conversation. The Kelley framework is academically sound, but the language of "exemplary follower" has a cultural problem in the fire service: it sounds like a boot-licker. It sounds like the person who agrees with everything and never pushes back. The cultural baggage around that term actively keeps people in the Survivor quadrant — because nobody wants to be seen as the one who drank the Kool-Aid.

So I changed the framing. What if instead of talking about exemplary followership, we talked about fandom?

Think about what a genuine fan looks like. Taylor Swift's Swifties. The Grateful Dead's Deadheads. Jimmy Buffett's Parrot Heads. The Barrel Man who painted himself orange for every Denver Broncos home game for decades. These people are not passive. They are not compliant. They are deeply engaged, highly independent in their enthusiasm, actively part of a community, and fiercely loyal to something they have chosen to be part of. Nobody calls a Swiftie a boot-licker. Nobody accuses a Deadhead of lacking critical thinking.

Now look at the four qualities that define fandom:

  • Passion and Commitment — genuine investment in something beyond personal gain
  • Active Engagement — participation, contribution, initiative, not passive receipt of information
  • Investment and Loyalty — resilience when things are hard, commitment through adversity
  • Community — camaraderie, shared purpose, belonging to something larger than yourself

Those four qualities describe the best crews in the fire service. They describe the shifts that run together well. They describe the people you want in the building when things go wrong. And they are identical — not similar, identical — to the qualities that define exemplary followership on the Kelley matrix.

The Question I Ask Every New Leader
You wear the jersey. You have the plates on your car. You go to the chili cook-off. You are a hero to kids who do not know your name. You get discounts on everything because of what you do. You never work a day in your life if you love what you signed up for. So why do so many people in our profession hate being here? And what is your role as a leader in changing that — not for everyone, but for the 29 people it takes to move a culture?

The Framing Effect — Why the Kool-Aid Metaphor Is Destroying Your Culture

Here is where the cognitive science comes in. The Framing Effect is a well-documented cognitive bias — it describes how the way information is presented (its "frame") profoundly influences the decisions people make about it, independent of the objective content. Daniel Kahneman's Nobel Prize-winning research on this is in your reading list. But applying it specifically to the followership problem in fire service culture reveals something important.

The phrase "drinking the Kool-Aid" has been negatively framed into our professional culture to the point where it invokes, immediately and almost universally, images of blind compliance and cult-like submission. (For the record, it was actually Flavor-Aid at Jonestown, not Kool-Aid — but accuracy has never stopped a good cultural narrative.) That framing creates a powerful de-incentive to move into the Exemplary Follower / Fan quadrant because the social cost of being labeled a Kool-Aid drinker is significant in a culture that values cynicism as a proxy for experience.

We call this the Kool-Aid Trap. And understand that this is not a harmless cultural quirk. It is a mechanism that actively keeps talented, capable, engaged people in the Survivor quadrant. It keeps your best potential leaders performing at 60% because performing at 100% gets you labeled as someone who drank the Kool-Aid.

🧠
The Framing Effect — Applied to Organizational Culture
Kahneman, 2011
The Framing Effect describes how identical information presented in different frames produces different decisions. In organizational culture, the frame that surrounds followership, engagement, and institutional loyalty determines whether capable people choose to invest themselves fully or protect themselves through strategic disengagement. When engagement is positively framed (fandom, belonging, passion, community) it is aspirational. When it is negatively framed (Kool-Aid, boot-licking, politicking) it is culturally toxic. The content of the behavior is the same. The frame changes everything about whether people choose to engage.
Your Leadership Application As a company officer, you are the primary framing agent for your crew. How you talk about the department, the mission, the administration, the culture — that is the frame through which your crew processes every piece of organizational information. You can be honest about problems and still maintain a positive frame. The difference is whether your language invites engagement or signals that disengagement is the sophisticated response.

Promotivation — The Word for What We Have All Been Watching

The term promotivation came out of our department culture to describe a specific phenomenon visible in every fire department: followership that is tied to the good times of an organizational timeline. The person who is enthusiastic, engaged, and positive when things are going well — when the budget is good, when the new equipment arrives, when the administration is popular — and who retreats to the Survivor quadrant the moment there is any friction, controversy, or adversity.

Promotivation is not bad character. It is a predictable response to psychological unsafety. When people do not know where the organization is going, who is driving, how long the journey is, or where they are on it — they protect themselves. They stop investing in outcomes they cannot see. They retreat to what Maslow would call the safety tier: if I cannot see my future here, I am not putting more of myself into it than I have to.

As a leader, your job is to close that information gap. The research on the Law of Diffusion of Innovation tells us that you do not need everyone in the fan quadrant to change a culture. You need 16%. In a department of 180 people, that is 29 individuals. Twenty-nine people who are genuinely in the upper-right quadrant — thinking independently, engaging actively, modeling what committed professionalism looks like — can shift the entire cultural gravity of an organization. That is not an inspirational statistic. That is operational math.

Promotivation is the symptom of psychological unsafety, not bad character. Close the information gap and you change the behavior. Your crew does not need certainty — they need a compass and a driver they can see.

Building Camelot:
The Culture Code & Personal Covenant

⏱ 26 min
🛡️ Values Architecture
📋 Personal Code exercise

Every great leadership culture is built on a story. Not a mission statement drafted by committee or a set of values selected because they tested well in a survey — a story. A narrative that gives people an identity, a history, and a reason to carry the standards even when no one is watching.

The culture in our EMS division is grounded in the mythology of Camelot — and before you dismiss that as EMS nerd stuff, consider this:

You Are Already Nerds
The Maltese Cross — the symbol of the modern fire service, on your badge, on your helmet, on your t-shirt — originated with the Knights of St. John, an organization founded in the 11th century to care for sick and poor pilgrims in Jerusalem. They later fought in the Crusades, where they were thrown into flaming torches after being struck by glass bombs filled with naphtha. Fellow crusaders awarded them the Maltese Cross as a badge of honor for their bravery under fire. The cross represents charity, chivalry, loyalty, and protection of the weak. You have been wearing a knight's medal since your first day on this job. The mythology was always here. We are just naming it.

The Round Table — What It Means and Why It Matters

King Arthur's Round Table was not a piece of furniture. It was an architectural statement about leadership philosophy. No head of table. No seat of power. Every chair equal. Every voice heard. Every person at the table a capable leader in their own right, contributing their specific and irreplaceable gifts to a mission none of them could accomplish alone.

That is the culture this module asks you to build and sustain. Not a hierarchy that enforces its authority through rank. Not a leadership culture that works because the boss says so. A round table — where the sum of all the parts is greater than any one of us individually, where cumulative intelligence and diverse capability are the actual source of organizational strength.

The inscription at the center of our Round Table culture is this: "In serving each other, we become free." Think about what that means. When you are genuinely committed to serving the people around you — your crew, your shift, your patients, your partners — you are freed from the exhausting performance of self-protection. You are freed from the politics of position. You are freed from the anxiety of whether people like you or respect you. The service itself is the answer to all of those questions.

King Arthur and the Burden of Leadership

Arthur is the prototype for servant leadership in Western mythology, and we use him deliberately because the story is both true and honest about its costs. Arthur did not ask to pull the sword from the stone. He was not campaigning for the role. He accepted the burden of leadership because it was asked of him and because the mission required someone to carry it.

That is what accepting rank at any level in this profession actually means. You are not signing up for authority. You are signing up for the weight. The unspoken and unwritten expectations that come with the role. The days when you are not allowed to have a bad day because your crew needs you steady. The decisions that cost you relationships. The calls you take home because they belong to you as the officer on scene.

The lesson we carry for every new leader: the rank is the sword. Pulling it changes everything. You did not earn it to carry it like a trophy. You earned it to carry it like a responsibility. The moment you start treating it like the former, you begin the kind of withdrawal from the leadership account that ends careers and damages crews.

Merlin — The Role of Mentorship

Arthur could not have built Camelot without Merlin. Merlin was the embodiment of institutional wisdom — the person who had seen enough to know what was coming, who understood the deeper forces at work, and who invested his knowledge in the leader who would carry the mission forward. Every experienced officer in your department who has invested in your development is your Merlin. Your job now is to become one for someone else. The Round Table multiplies when the people at it develop the next generation of people who deserve to sit there.

The Knights and What They Teach

The Knights of the Round Table were not uniform. Each one brought a specific virtue and a specific weakness. That is what makes the mythology useful for leadership teaching — it is honest about the complexity of trying to be excellent while remaining human.

  • Lancelot — the paragon of chivalry and the cautionary tale about pride. The greatest knight in the kingdom, whose unchecked passion ultimately fractured the very institution he loved. What does the best version of you look like when you lead from your strengths without checking your blind spots?
  • Gawain — the knight of courtesy and loyalty, whose encounter with the Green Knight tested whether he would maintain his integrity when survival was on the line. Where is your Green Knight scenario — the moment when your integrity will cost you something real?
  • Galahad — the pure and spiritual knight who achieved the Holy Grail not through power but through moral clarity. Not everyone will achieve what Galahad achieved. But every team needs someone who sets the standard so high that everyone else rises to be near it.
  • Percival — the compassionate knight whose empathy and humility allowed him to ask the question no one else thought to ask. In EMS and fire leadership, the ability to see what your patient — or your crew member — actually needs, rather than what you assume they need, is often the difference between genuine service and performance of service.
  • Bedivere — the loyal and steadfast knight who fulfilled his duty at the moment it cost him the most. The last one standing. The one who did what was asked of him even when the kingdom was falling. That kind of loyalty — to the mission, to the standard, to each other — is what makes a culture durable.

The Chivalric Code — Translated to Fire/EMS Leadership

The knights operated by a code. Not rules — a code. Rules tell you what you cannot do. A code tells you who you are. I translated the Chivalric Code into the language of fire/EMS leadership, and then I built a personal covenant on top of it for our division. Here is both.

⚔️

The Personal Covenant — T·C·H·O·E·D·S

This is the code I ask every leader in our division to adopt. Not memorize — adopt. There is a difference. Memorization is recitation. Adoption is the decision to be accountable to this set of principles in your actual behavior, in the actual difficult moments, when no one is grading you and the easier path is available.

Seven pillars. No head of table. Every one of them necessary:

T
Trust
We will trust the vision for where we are going and align our mission and values to help realize that future. We will trust each other always — not naively, but as a default that we only revise when evidence specifically and clearly requires it. We will err on the side of trusting our people no matter the situation. And we will trust our plan while remaining flexible enough to navigate the turbulence we will encounter on the way.
C
Courage
We will always have the courage to do and say what is right — especially when it is the most difficult path available. We will stand up for the people above us and beside us. We will have the hard conversations and do the hard work when others will not. Courage in leadership is not absence of fear. It is the decision to act in alignment with your values regardless of the cost.
H
Humility
We will always remain humble and know that what we accomplish is because of the team. We do not exist for personal accolades or gain but to elevate the people around us and the organization we serve. We will find our gratification through the achievements of our people — because we understand that if our people succeed, our citizens will receive the best care and service we have to offer.
O
Ownership
We will take full responsibility for our team, our mission, our values, and our outcomes — wins and failures alike. We will not deflect, minimize, or point elsewhere when things go wrong. We will strive to create a culture of accountability where everyone — at every level — operates with the same standard. Ownership is not blame. It is the decision to be the solution rather than the observer of the problem.
E
Empathy
We understand that empathy is integral to building trust, creating meaningful connection, and cultivating a culture worth being part of. We will actively listen, show genuine concern, and put ourselves in others' situations before making decisions that affect them. This is not weakness — it is precision. Leaders who operate without empathy make decisions that are technically correct and humanly wrong. We will not be those leaders.
D
Dedication
We understand that dedication is the foundation on which everything else is built. It is the commitment that carries through the hard stretches, the adversity, the seasons when motivation is unavailable and discipline is the only thing that keeps the standard intact. We will demonstrate dedication consistently — not performatively. The standard you maintain when no one is watching is the real standard you hold.
S
Service
Service is at the absolute core of who we are and what we do. Not service as performance. Not service when it is convenient or when the recipient deserves it by our estimation. Service as identity — to our citizens, our internal and external partners, and most importantly to each other. In the words that land every time we say them: "I have served. I will be of service." Will you?
The Contradiction Test — For Your Oral Board and Your Career
Every element of the T·C·H·O·E·D·S code is an oral board dimension in disguise. When assessors ask about a time you showed courage under pressure — that is C. When they ask how you handled a team failure — that is O and H. When they probe your leadership philosophy in a supplemental question — they are checking whether the code you present matches the behavior you demonstrate. The candidates who score highest are the ones whose presentation and their behavioral examples tell the same story. Start building that story now, in your Field Record, before your assessment day arrives.

The Maltese Cross is a knight's medal. The mythology was always here. Great leaders name the culture they are building, rather than leaving it to form by accident.

Rank is the sword. You pulled it. That means you carry the weight — the unwritten expectations, the days when you are not allowed to fall apart, the decisions that cost you something. That is what you signed up for.

The Wizard of Oz,
MVV, and Your Leadership Style

⏱ 22 min
🧠 Framework + Self-Assessment
📋 Style inventory included

After a lot of leadership books, a lot of leadership programs, and more hours of leadership training than anyone cares to count, the clearest, most memorable illustration of Mission, Vision, and Values remains a 1939 MGM musical about a girl from Kansas trying to get home.

The Wizard of Oz is the greatest leadership movie ever made. Here is the case for it — in about four minutes.

The MVV Framework — Through the Lens of Oz

Mission — Follow the Yellow Brick Road. The mission answers the question: why do we exist? It is the active purpose, the thing you are doing right now, the road under your feet. Dorothy's mission is not abstract — it is concrete, directional, and immediately actionable. Follow this road. In fire/EMS, the mission is equally concrete: we exist to quickly mitigate the emergent needs of the people who call us. Every decision on every shift should be traceable back to that road.

Vision — There's No Place Like Home. The vision answers: where are we going? It is the destination on the horizon — not the road, but what the road leads to. Dorothy's vision is emotionally specific, personally resonant, and worth every piece of difficulty on the journey. A good organizational vision does the same thing. It is not "achieve excellence" — it is a picture of a specific future state compelling enough that people will walk through a haunted forest to get there.

Values — The Four Companions. Values are the guiding principles that shape decisions along the road. They are not the road and they are not the destination — they are the qualities that determine how you travel. Dorothy's four companions are not incidental. Courage (the Cowardly Lion) — the willingness to act in the presence of fear. Compassion (Dorothy) — genuine care for those around you, even when you are the one who needs help. Intelligence (the Scarecrow) — the ability to think clearly and analytically under pressure. Heart (the Tin Man) — the emotional capacity to feel the weight of what you do and why it matters. Put those four qualities on a crew and you have something that can defeat a wicked witch and a flying monkey battalion.

What We Would Ask the Wizard For

When this module was developed for our division, every leader was asked one question: if you could ask the Wizard of Oz for one thing for your organization, what would it be? Here is our answer — not as a template, but as an example of what a specific, authentic vision looks like rather than a generic one.

A culture of mutual respect where individuals genuinely care for both the fire and EMS sides of what we do. Not performance of respect — the real kind, where the firefighter who does not personally love EMS still values, supports, and uplifts the people who do. Where those with a passion for EMS can pursue it without constant cultural friction. And a culture of total ownership — not just the parts of the job that are exciting or visible, but the parts that nobody wants. The documentation. The quality review. The difficult follow-up conversation after a bad call. That kind of ownership is what makes an organization genuinely excellent rather than occasionally impressive.

That is specific. That is personal. That is a vision that a department can actually lead toward. Generic vision statements do not produce extraordinary organizations — specific, authentic, personally owned visions do.

Understanding Your Leadership Style — And When to Use Each One

No single leadership style is correct. The research is unambiguous on this: the leaders who produce the best outcomes across the greatest range of situations are those who have developed fluency in multiple styles and the judgment to know which one a given situation requires.

There are four styles you need to understand and be able to deploy:

Servant Leadership. The foundation. Leaders exist to support the team and the mission. Communication is open. Listening is genuine. Empathy and compassion are operational values, not aspirational ones. This style builds the culture, creates the loyalty, and develops the next generation. Famous servant leaders: Abraham Lincoln, Gandhi, Martin Luther King Jr. The fire service talks about servant leadership more than it practices it. You should be an exception to that pattern.

Transformational Leadership. Servant leadership's partner in building an extraordinary culture. Transformational leaders are visionary communicators who challenge their teams intellectually, attend to individual developmental needs, and inspire performance by connecting the work to meaning rather than compliance. The Four I's from the research: Idealized Influence, Inspirational Motivation, Intellectual Stimulation, Individualized Consideration. Walt Disney built a theme park on this style. Bill Gates built an industry on it. Your company officers build lasting crews on it.

Visionary Leadership. Strategic thinking, clear communication of direction, calculated risk tolerance, and the resilience to maintain commitment to the vision through adversity. Steve Jobs is the iconic example. In the fire service, visionary leadership is most visible at the chief officer level — but company officers who operate with a clear personal vision for their crew are already practicing it at the right scale.

Situational Leadership. The practical master skill. The ability to read a person, a situation, and a moment — and deploy the style that the specific combination requires. A new probationary firefighter on their first month needs directing. A three-year veteran on a new assignment needs coaching. A ten-year expert on a familiar task needs supporting. A proven senior member who owns their role needs delegating. The situational leader is fluent in all four and defaults to none of them.

The Fire Service Leadership Style Problem
Research consistently shows that fire service leadership culture skews heavily toward directing and transactional approaches — set the expectation, monitor compliance, correct deviation. Those are necessary skills. They are not sufficient skills. The styles most underrepresented in fire service oral board responses are coaching and supporting — the ones that require genuine attention to the individual, genuine investment in their development, and genuine willingness to subordinate your approach to what they actually need. Those are the answers that score at 4 and 5. Practice them first.

The best leaders share one quality above all others: they are honest about how their own thinking works — including the ways it fails them. This lesson is about giving you the tools that make good decisions more likely and the self-awareness to recognize when your instincts are leading you wrong.

Leadership Matrices — Visual Tools for Sound Decisions

A matrix is not bureaucracy. It is clarity. It is a way of organizing competing variables so that your decision-making is driven by the actual situation rather than by whoever presented the problem most energetically, or most recently, or most loudly. Every company officer should have three matrices working in their decision toolkit at all times.

The Eisenhower Matrix — Prioritization Under Load

President Eisenhower's framework sorts tasks into four quadrants by two variables: urgency (does this require action now?) and importance (does this actually matter to the mission?). The resulting four categories: Do First (urgent and important — crises, true deadlines, genuine emergencies); Schedule (important but not urgent — long-term planning, relationship building, professional development, the things that produce the most value but never scream at you); Delegate (urgent but not important — the interruptions, the administrative requests, the tasks that need to happen but do not need to happen by you); and Eliminate (not urgent and not important — the time drains, the distraction, the organizational noise that consumes hours without producing anything).

The most important insight from the Eisenhower Matrix is not the Do First quadrant — that one takes care of itself. It is the Schedule quadrant. The important, non-urgent work is the work that transforms organizations. Leadership development. Strategic planning. Culture-building. Relationship investment. This is the work that never has a deadline and therefore never gets done unless you protect it deliberately. The leaders who spend most of their time in the Do First quadrant are reactive. The leaders who carve protected time for the Schedule quadrant are transformational.

The Skill/Will Matrix — Diagnosing What Your People Need

Every person on your crew exists somewhere on a 2×2 grid of skill (can they do the task?) and will (do they want to do the task?). The quadrant they occupy determines the supervisory response — and using the wrong response for the wrong quadrant is one of the most common and most damaging supervisory errors in the fire service.

📊
Skill/Will Matrix — Quick Assessment and Response Guide
Supervisory Diagnostic
High Skill / High Will — Top performers ready for expanded responsibility. Response: promote, delegate, develop for leadership. The most common supervisory error here is hovering — micro-managing a capable, motivated person destroys their engagement and burns down your credibility faster than almost any other behavior. High Will / Low Skill — Enthusiastic but undertrained. Response: train, educate, and develop. The energy is the asset — channel it. Low Will / High Skill — Capable but checked out. Response: diagnose first. Is this boredom? Unmet expectation? Organizational disillusionment? The intervention varies dramatically based on the cause. Low Will / Low Skill — Require significant support and honest assessment of fit. This is the quadrant where most supervisors spend disproportionate time and energy — and where the Pareto Principle has something important to say.
The Oral Board Application When asked about a personnel management scenario, identify the skill/will quadrant of the person in the scenario before deciding your response. Assessors are listening to determine whether your supervisory response matches the actual situation or whether you are applying a default approach regardless of context. "I would have a coaching conversation" is the wrong answer when the behavior is a will problem, not a skill problem. Name the distinction.

The Pareto Principle — Where to Invest Your Leadership Energy

The 80/20 rule in leadership: roughly 80% of your organizational outputs come from 20% of your people. Most organizational energy is spent trying to move the 80% — the average and below-average performers — while the 20% who produce most of the value are neglected, underdeveloped, and eventually either leave or stop trying.

I am not suggesting you abandon the 80%. Every member of your crew deserves your attention and your investment. What I am suggesting is that most supervisors systematically underinvest in their best people because those people do not generate problems that demand attention. They just quietly outperform everyone and wait for someone to notice. When that recognition and investment never comes, they stop waiting. The Pareto insight is not about abandoning the many — it is about making sure you are not neglecting the few who are already showing you exactly the culture you want to build.

Emotional Intelligence — The Competency That Predicts Everything Else

Daniel Goleman's research on emotional intelligence identified four domains — Self-Awareness, Self-Management, Social Awareness, and Relationship Management — as the primary predictors of leadership effectiveness across industries, roles, and cultures. In the fire service specifically, where the work involves life and death decisions made in seconds under conditions of extreme physiological and psychological stress, emotional intelligence is not a soft skill. It is the hardware the job runs on.

Self-Awareness is the foundation: do you know how you are feeling, how that feeling is affecting your judgment, and how you are coming across to the people around you? In a high-stress incident, the leader who does not know they are frightened makes decisions driven by concealed fear. The leader who knows they are frightened can make decisions despite it.

Self-Management is the ability to regulate your emotional response — to feel the anger, the fear, the frustration, and choose your behavioral response rather than simply executing the emotional impulse. This is the skill that determines whether a difficult crew member conversation becomes a productive coaching session or a fireable offense.

Social Awareness — what Goleman calls empathy at scale — is the ability to read the room: your crew's emotional state, the patient's family's experience, the tension in a multi-agency response, the political environment in your administration. The company officer who is socially unaware is constantly surprised by things that everyone else saw coming.

Relationship Management is the ability to use your self-awareness, self-management, and social awareness to build, repair, maintain, and leverage relationships in service of the mission. This is where the Bank of Leadership, the Round Table culture, and the T·C·H·O·E·D·S code all live in practice. It is the application layer of everything else in this module.

Unconscious Bias — The Enemy You Cannot See Coming

Unconscious biases are learned stereotypes that operate outside of your awareness. That is not a political statement — it is a cognitive neuroscience finding. Every one of us has them, regardless of our values, our intentions, or our commitment to fairness. They are the product of pattern-matching systems in our brains that operate faster than conscious thought and that use statistical heuristics — generalizations about categories — to make rapid judgments.

In supervisory decisions, unconscious bias affects who gets promoted, who gets the good assignments, who gets the benefit of the doubt when something goes wrong, and whose concerns get taken seriously. The fact that these effects occur outside conscious awareness does not make them less real or less consequential. It makes them more dangerous — because the leader who believes they are unbiased is the one who never examines their decisions for bias.

The practice is not to be unbiased — that is not achievable. The practice is to build decisions that require accountability to criteria rather than impression. Write down what you are looking for before you evaluate. Apply the same standard explicitly to everyone. Ask yourself: would I make the same decision if this person were different in this specific way? The friction of that question is not political correctness. It is supervisory rigor.

A Closing Word — From One Practitioner to Another

Everything in this module was field-tested before it was written down. The Bank of Leadership metaphor came from watching what actually happened to leaders who made deposits consistently versus those who spent everything they had. The Camelot framework came from needing a story that a crew of firefighters and medics could adopt as their own — not because they were told to, but because it genuinely described something they recognized as true. The T·C·H·O·E·D·S code came from asking what I actually wanted to be accountable to, not what sounded good on a slide.

What I am asking you to do with this module is the same thing: take what fits, make it yours, and hold yourself accountable to it in the actual moments when it costs you something. The leadership philosophy that only appears in your oral board and disappears the moment you have the rank is not a philosophy — it is a performance. The fire service does not need more performances. It needs more leaders who have decided who they are going to be before the pressure arrives to make that decision for them.
